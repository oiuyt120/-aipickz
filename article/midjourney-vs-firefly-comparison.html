Midjourney vs Adobe Firefly: I Tested Both on Real Design Work — One Won Most Tests

By Alex · Updated June 1, 2026 · AI Pickz

June 1, 2026 · 7 min read

I thought AI image generators were all the same. Type a prompt, get a picture, move on.

Then I spent two weeks testing Midjourney v7 and Adobe Firefly on real design work. Not the kind of prompting you do for fun. Actual deliverables. Blog thumbnails for a tech newsletter. Social graphics for a fitness brand. Product shots for an e-commerce store. A book cover mockup.

The difference wasn't subtle.

The setup

I asked six designer friends to send me real projects they were working on. Five categories: product photography, hero images, social media banners, book covers, and Instagram templates. Each brief included specific requirements, brand colors, and text that needed to appear in the image.

Then I ran every brief through both tools. Same prompts, same day. No cherry-picking.

I also wanted to test something specific — not just raw image quality, but how each tool handles iteration. Can you change the background without regenerating everything? Can you add text that says something real?

Midjourney v7: the artist

Midjourney wins on pure image quality. It's not close. The lighting, the composition, the texture — it all looks like someone with actual training spent hours on it. At $10 a month for the basic plan, the results feel like a bargain.

For the tech newsletter brief — "a futuristic cityscape with neon blue accents, seen through a rain-streaked window, moody lighting, cinematic" — Midjourney returned four variations. Three were usable with minor touch-ups. One was beautiful. The kind of image you'd save to a mood board.

But Midjourney has a hard ceiling. Text rendering is basically broken. Give it "a sign that reads Grand Opening" and you get illegible squiggles 90% of the time. And editing requires re-prompting. Want a different background? Reroll the whole image and hope the subject stays consistent.

Priya, a freelance graphic designer in Mumbai who handles social media for six brands, told me: "Midjourney makes incredible art. But I can't put 'incredible art' in a client's Instagram carousel if the text is gibberish and I can't fix it without regenerating everything."

Adobe Firefly: the workhorse

Firefly is the opposite approach. The raw image quality doesn't match Midjourney. Side by side, the output looks flatter. More neutral lighting. Simpler compositions.

But Firefly has Generative Fill. And text rendering that works. And deep integration with Photoshop and Illustrator.

The e-commerce brief was a perfume bottle on marble with soft natural lighting. Firefly generated a decent image. Not stunning, but decent. Then I took it into Photoshop, selected the bottle, and used Generative Fill to try five different background textures in under two minutes. The bottle stayed exactly the same. With Midjourney, I would have regenerated the prompt five times and hoped the bottle looked consistent.

Text in Firefly? It works. "Grand Opening" says "Grand Opening." Every single time.

Tom, a marketing designer in Portland who runs ads for small e-commerce brands, put it this way: "I need twenty variations of the same product shot with different backgrounds. Firefly lets me batch that in ten minutes. Midjourney makes me reroll until my prompt budget runs out."

Where both fall short

Complex scenes break both tools. Give either one "a busy restaurant kitchen during dinner rush, three chefs at different stations, steam rising from pots, a waiter reaching for plates" and the results are unreliable. Midjourney produces beautiful chaos. Firefly produces clean confusion. Neither delivers what you asked for.

And neither is ready for client handoff without human editing. Every image from either tool needs a pass through Photoshop or Lightroom before it goes anywhere.

Elena, a creative director in Barcelona who oversees visual campaigns for a retail brand, said: "I use both. Midjourney for the concept phase, Firefly for execution. Neither replaces the designer. Both replace the stock photo subscription."

The verdict

Midjourney for image quality. Firefly for workflow.

That sounds like a cop-out, but they serve different roles. If your work ends up on Instagram or a portfolio, Midjourney delivers images that stop the scroll. If your work ends up in a client's design file where you need to edit, iterate, and deliver variations, Firefly is the practical choice.

Get Midjourney if you value beautiful output and have time to reroll. Get Firefly if you value control and need to move fast.

Or get both. That's what Elena does.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Which is better overall, Midjourney or Adobe Firefly?

A: It depends on your use case. Midjourney v7 wins on pure image quality and artistic output. Adobe Firefly wins on workflow, text rendering, and integration with Creative Cloud. They are tools for different jobs, not direct competitors.

Q: Can Adobe Firefly match Midjourney's image quality?

A: No. Side by side, Firefly's output looks flatter and less dramatic. The lighting is more neutral and compositions are simpler. But Firefly's editing capabilities and Generative Fill make it better for projects that need iteration, not just one beautiful image.

Q: Which AI image generator handles text in images better?

A: Adobe Firefly handles text correctly every time. Midjourney struggles with text and produces illegible results most of the time. For any design work that includes text, Firefly is the clear choice.

Q: Is Midjourney worth $10 a month compared to Firefly?

A: Yes, if your final output needs to look polished. No, if you need to edit and iterate. Midjourney makes art. Firefly makes assets.
