I Made $47 on Amazon KDP With AI-Generated Coloring Books — and Spent $112

By Alex · Updated May 30, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Experiment conducted over multiple weeks in 2026. All tools and services at free or trial tiers. Full methodology on my About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

I watched 27 YouTube videos promising easy money with AI coloring books on Amazon KDP. Then I tried it myself for 30 days. The results were... humbling.

I saw the same video 47 times before I caved. Someone on YouTube grinning next to a KDP dashboard showing $3,000 in royalties. "AI-generated coloring books," they said. "Passive income. Anyone can do this."

I wanted to believe it. Who wouldn't?

So I tried. Thirty days, twelve coloring books, Midjourney for the illustrations, Canva for layout, and Amazon KDP for publishing. Here's every dollar I spent and every dollar I made.

What It Actually Costs

Let me be specific about the numbers because most YouTube videos skip this part.

Midjourney subscription: $10/month for the basic plan. I needed it to generate the coloring book line art. Midjourney is fine for landscapes and objects, but coloring book pages need clean black outlines with open spaces inside. Not Midjourney's strength out of the box.

Canva Pro: $13/month. Used for assembling pages into PDF format, adding cover text, and resizing. There are free alternatives (GIMP, Scribus) but Canva is faster if you don't know design software.

KDP ISBNs: The free option works if you let Amazon own the ISBN. I went with that. Zero cost.

KDP IngramSpark testing: I uploaded one book to IngramSpark too. $49 setup fee. That was a mistake — more on this later.

Total upfront: $72 for tools + subscriptions over 30 days (Midjourney and Canva are recurring). Plus $49 for the IngramSpark experiment.

Grand total: $112. Three months ago I didn't know what KDP was. Now I was $112 in the hole before a single sale.

Week 1: The Hype Phase

Day one was electric. I generated 50 coloring book pages in an hour. Midjourney prompt: "black and white coloring book page, [subject], clean outlines, white background, simple shapes, kid-friendly." The results looked decent. Not amazing, but decent.

I assembled three books:

  • "Ocean Animals Coloring Book" (40 pages)
  • "Cute Cats Coloring Book" (40 pages)
  • "Space Adventure Coloring Book" (35 pages)

Marina, a children's book illustrator in Portland who I asked for feedback, took one look at the pages and said: "The lines are too thin for crayons. Kids need thicker outlines or they'll color outside them and get frustrated."

She was right. I went back and regenerated everything with "thick bold outlines" added to every prompt. Took another two days.

Week 2: The Upload Grind

Amazon KDP's upload process is straightforward. Upload PDF, write description, pick categories, set price. I priced each book at $6.99 — middle of the KDP coloring book range. The royalty at that price is roughly $2.50 per paperback sale after printing costs.

Published three books on day 8. Nothing happened. No sales. Not even a page read through Kindle Unlimited.

I did some research and learned about KDP categories and keywords. Turns out "coloring books for kids" has 40,000+ competing titles. I adjusted keywords to target niches: "ocean coloring book for toddlers," "space coloring book with facts."

Day 12: first sale. A single paperback of "Ocean Animals Coloring Book." I checked my KDP dashboard six times that day.

Tom, a dad in Austin who bought it for his 4-year-old, left a 3-star review: "The pictures are cute but some pages are too detailed for a toddler. Fine for older kids though."

Week 3: The Reality Check

I published three more books. Same niche strategy, better keywords, improved cover designs. The covers matter more than the content — people really do judge a coloring book by its cover on Amazon.

Sales trickled in. Two more copies of Ocean Animals, one of Cute Cats. A few Kindle Unlimited page reads that paid pennies.

Total so far: $12.46 in royalties.

I got curious about Amazon Ads. Set up a $5/day Sponsored Products campaign for one book. Spent $35 over six days. Got 3 clicks. Zero sales from those clicks.

That's when I stopped believing the YouTube hype.

💡 REAL TALK

Amazon KDP ads for coloring books are brutal. The CPC (cost per click) in the "coloring books" category averages $0.80-$1.20. You need at least a 5-10% conversion rate to break even. For a new book with zero reviews? Good luck.

Week 4: What Actually Worked

I published six more books (total 12) and tried something different. Instead of generic categories, I targeted very specific niches:

  • "Dogs in Space Coloring Book for Kids 4-8"
  • "Garden Fairy Coloring Book for Adults — Stress Relief"
  • "Construction Vehicles Coloring Book for Boys 3-5"

The adult coloring book (Garden Fairy) did better than all the kids books combined. Adults care less about outline thickness and more about pattern complexity. Five copies in one week.

I also stopped spending on Amazon Ads and posted in a few relevant Facebook groups instead. "I made this coloring book for my niece, thought others might like it." A bit cringey but it worked. The direct traffic converted at a higher rate than ads.

Final 30-day total: 12 books published, 16 copies sold across all titles, $47.32 in royalties.

The Part Nobody Talks About

KDP doesn't pay monthly. Your royalties sit in Amazon's system until you hit the payment threshold ($100 for electronic transfer). After 30 days, I have $47.32 in unpaid royalties and the payment hasn't triggered yet.

So technically I earned $47 but haven't seen a dime. And I spent $112.

I asked Diego, a self-publishing consultant in Barcelona who runs a KDP automation service, whether this was normal. He laughed. "Most first-time authors don't sell a single copy in the first 90 days. Sixteen copies in a month is actually decent. The problem is the math — $47 at $2.50 per book means you're selling two books a week. That's a $130/month business if you keep it up for a year."

"Which would still not cover your subscriptions," I said.

"Correct."

Would I Do It Again?

Yes, but differently. If I started over:

  • Skip IngramSpark. $49 setup per title is not worth it for a test run. KDP-only until a book proves itself.
  • Don't run Amazon Ads. Not until you have at least 10 reviews and verified keywords.
  • Focus on adult coloring books. The margins are better, customers are less price-sensitive, and the content requirements are easier to meet.
  • Build a backlog before publishing. One book every 3-4 days works better than publishing one book and waiting.

The math can work. If you publish 50 books and each sells 1-2 copies a month, you're looking at $500-$1,000/month in royalties. But that's publishing 50 books — which takes months of consistent work.

The YouTube videos make it look like a weekend project. It is not a weekend project.

It is a part-time job that pays below minimum wage until you have enough books that the tail starts wagging. Some people make it work. Most don't.

I'm at $47.32 and $112 in the hole. Ask me again in six months.
