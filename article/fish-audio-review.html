Fish Audio Review: I Didn't Think a $14 TTS Tool Could Beat ElevenLabs. Then I Listened.

By Alex · Updated June 3, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Standard plan tested of Fish Audio over multiple days. Full methodology on my About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

I figured good AI voice cloning required a credit card with a three-digit limit. ElevenLabs charges $22 for its basic plan. Respeecher bills per minute. Even the "cheap" options hover around $20/month.

Then someone in a voice production Discord dropped a link to Fish Audio. "It's $14," they said. "And honestly? It sounds better."

I did not believe them.

The skeptic test

Fish Audio is an open-source TTS platform from China. It started as Fish Speech, a research model that ranked high on the TTS leaderboard. The commercial product launched with a simple claim: pay less, get more.

That claim usually means corners got cut. Voice cloning is hard. Natural intonation across multiple languages is harder. I expected robotic delivery, garbled Chinese-to-English accents, latency that makes the ElevenLabs API feel snappy.

I signed up for the $14 Creator plan. 200 minutes of voice generation. Voice cloning included. No credit card required to start. That was the first surprise.

What I tested

Three voiceover tasks I needed done:

Task one: A 60-second product explainer video, male narrator, English.

Task two: A 30-second Mandarin voiceover for a Chinese social media clip.

Task three: Clone my own voice from a 3-minute recording, then generate a 2-minute blog narration.

I ran the same three tasks through ElevenLabs (my $22 Turbo plan) as a baseline. No fine-tuning either tool. Just prompt, generate, download.

The English voice — shockingly close

First pass on the English explainer: I used Fish Audio's library voice "Alex." The first generation had a slight metallic edge in the mids. Not terrible, but you'd hear it on a podcast.

I adjusted the speed slider down by 5% and ran it again. The second pass was clean. Warm. The kind of voice you'd hire for a Kickstarter video.

Same script in ElevenLabs with their "Adam" voice. Adam sounded slightly more polished: richer lows, smoother transitions between words. But we're talking 10% better, not 2x better. The $8 price difference per month does not feel proportionally reflected in the output.

Luca, a video editor in Milan who runs a faceless YouTube channel, told me he switched to Fish Audio three months ago. "I was on ElevenLabs for eight months. I cannot hear the difference in my final cut. I can hear the $8 in my bank account."

The Mandarin voice — this shifted my stance

English TTS is everywhere. Good Mandarin TTS is rare. Most tools either sound like a GPS navigation unit or make tonal mistakes that change word meanings.

Fish Audio's Chinese voice "叶琳" delivered the 30-second clip with correct tones. It hit the third-tone dips where they needed to be. The pacing felt natural, not rushed.

ElevenLabs does have Mandarin support now, but the voice choices are limited and the cadence felt... translated. Like a Mandarin speaker reading an English sentence structure.

This is where Fish Audio pulled ahead. It trained on significantly more Chinese data. Makes sense. The research team is based in Beijing.

Voice cloning — the trade-off

I uploaded a 3-minute recording of myself reading a script. Fish Audio processed it in about 40 seconds.

The first clone generation was... okay. It captured my pacing but flattened the pitch variation. I sound like a slightly depressed version of myself.

ElevenLabs' voice cloning captured more of my natural intonation. That upward lift at the end of questions? ElevenLabs got it. Fish Audio smoothed it out.

The fix: I fed Fish Audio a second sample: a more animated recording with wider pitch range. The second clone was noticeably better. Not ElevenLabs good, but good enough for narration.

For professional dubbing or character voices, ElevenLabs stays ahead. For blog narration, YouTube voiceovers, and internal training videos? Fish Audio gets you there for less.

The open-source advantage

Fish Audio runs on Fish Speech 1.5, which is fully open-source. The model weights are on HuggingFace. You can self-host if you have the GPU.

This matters if you are building a product that needs TTS at scale. No vendor lock-in. No surprise API deprecation. You can replicate the results locally if the cloud pricing ever changes.

Dan, a developer building a language learning app in Berlin, runs Fish Speech on a single RTX 4090. "I generate about 10,000 sentences a week for pronunciation exercises. ElevenLabs API would cost me around $400 a month. Self-hosting Fish cost me a one-time GPU purchase."

What still annoys me

The web UI is functional but ugly. It looks like a research project that escaped the lab. ElevenLabs' interface is slick. Fish Audio's feels like a Bootstrap demo from 2018.

The voice library is smaller. ElevenLabs has hundreds of curated voices. Fish Audio has maybe 60. The quality varies more: some community voices are excellent, some were trained on a phone call.

And the English voices still have that faint "synthetic" quality on certain phonemes. "S" sounds hiss slightly. "R" sounds can feel rounded. It's subtle but it's there.

Who should buy it

Get Fish Audio if you need multilingual TTS on a budget, especially if you work with Chinese, Japanese, or Korean content. The Asian language quality beats the competition.

Buy if you are a developer who wants to self-host and avoid per-character pricing.

Buy if your use case is YouTube narration, training videos, audiobooks, or anything that does not require Hollywood-grade character voices.

Skip if you need ElevenLabs-level voice cloning precision for branded character voices. Skip if a polished UI matters to your workflow. Skip if "s" hiss drives you crazy.

I started this test expecting to laugh at a $14 tool. I am ending it with a second subscription active.
