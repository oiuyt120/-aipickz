I Dismissed These 5 AI Tools After One Try. I Was Wrong About All of Them.

Published June 2, 2026 · 7 min read

Five tools. Five quick judgments. Five write-offs.

I opened each one, spent five minutes clicking around, and decided they weren't for me. Didn't fit my workflow. Didn't solve a real problem. Overhyped.

A month later, I came back to all five. Here's where I was wrong.

Claude: too wordy, until I needed a second brain

My first impression of Claude was straightforward: it writes too much. Ask a question, get an essay back. I wanted concise. Claude wanted thorough.

Then I found Projects.

Now it's where I dump messy thinking. Drafts that don't make sense. Half-baked spec ideas. Research notes I haven't organized. Claude stitches them into something coherent. Not perfect. Better than the blank page I was staring at.

"You have to use it wrong first," says Tom, a product manager in Seattle. He spent three weeks hating Claude before it clicked. "Once you stop treating it like ChatGPT, it becomes something else entirely."

I still keep ChatGPT open for quick answers. But the messy thinking goes to Claude.

Verdict: Great for thinking out loud. Terrible if you need a one-sentence answer.

Canva Magic Studio: I called it a toy. Then a client needed graphics by tomorrow.

Canva Magic Studio looked like a design tool for people who don't design. Templates, AI generation, drag and drop. Nothing wrong with that. I just didn't think it was for me.

A client asked for six social graphics by the next morning. I didn't have time to open Figma. Magic Studio's AI generated layouts from a text prompt. I picked one, tweaked the colors, exported. Twenty minutes, six graphics.

"I wouldn't use it for a brand deck," says Maya, a graphic designer in Austin. She keeps Canva as her backup. "For social posts and quick mockups, it's faster than anything else I've tried."

Verdict: Perfect for quick turnarounds. The AI layouts can feel generic. Customize them and they work fine.

Reclaim AI: calendar scheduling is boring. Until you miss a deadline.

Reclaim AI calls itself a smart calendar. It auto-schedules tasks, blocks focus time, and deflects meetings. I saw "meeting scheduler" and moved on.

Then I double-booked a Tuesday. Two client calls, one internal sync, and a deadline I'd forgotten. Reclaim spotted the conflict, moved the lower-priority task, blocked an hour for the deadline. I did nothing.

Elena, a freelance consultant in Chicago, told me she fought Reclaim for two weeks. "I didn't trust an AI to touch my calendar. Now I let it schedule everything except lunch."

Verdict: Excellent for people with packed calendars. If you have three meetings a week, skip it.

Tabby: a Copilot clone that worked when nothing else did

Tabby is open-source AI code completion. I tried it once, found the suggestions slower than Copilot, and closed it.

A month later, I was on a flight with no internet. Copilot froze. Cursor degraded. Tabby, running locally, kept suggesting.

"Tabby's not the best when you have perfect internet," says James, a backend developer in Berlin. "When you're offline or on a VPN, it's the only one that still works."

The suggestions aren't smarter than Copilot's. But they're there. Every time.

Verdict: Keep it installed for offline work. Don't use it as your primary when you have internet.

Rytr: I expected garbage. It's fine for simple writing.

Rytr costs $9 a month. Half the price of Jasper. A third of Copy.ai. I figured the quality matched the price.

It doesn't. The output is solid for short-form writing. Email drafts. Blog intros. Social media captions. Nothing that wows you. Nothing that makes you cringe either.

"I use it for my weekly newsletter," says Priya, a startup marketer in Bangalore. "Rytr writes the first draft, I rewrite 40% of it. That still saves me two hours."

Verdict: Buy it if you write short things regularly. Avoid it for long-form or anything that needs a distinct voice.

Five tools. Five second chances. I'm glad I gave them.

The lesson is boring but true: first impressions are unreliable, especially with tools built to be used differently than you expect. The ones I kept weren't the most impressive on day one. They were the ones I needed on day thirty.
