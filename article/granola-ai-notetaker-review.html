The AI Notetaker That Doesn't Join Your Meetings. I Tested Granola for a Week.

By Alex · Updated May 31, 2026 · AI Pickz

Every AI meeting notetaker promises the same thing: "Never take notes again."

But here's what happens. The bot joins your call. People get weird about it. The transcript comes back with 47 "ums" and "ahs" per minute. And when you search for that one decision from last week, the answer sits somewhere between "Let me share my screen" and "Can everyone see that?"

I tried three of them before I found one that worked differently.

What I was doing before

Otter was my default for six months. It joined every meeting automatically. The live transcription was impressive. But the summaries? They read like someone transcribed a conversation, ran it through a generic summarizer, and called it done. Action items were often hallucinated. "Sounds like you should follow up on that" became a to-do item three times a week.

Fireflies was better on the summary front. But having a bot in every meeting felt invasive. Clients would ask "who joined?" and I'd explain that no, it's just an AI, and yes, it records everything. The awkwardness never went away.

What I wanted was something that took notes the way I do. One that focused on what mattered, skipped the filler, and captured the decisions I care about.

How Granola works

Granola does one thing differently. It doesn't join your meeting.

You install the Mac app. It listens to your microphone locally. While the meeting runs, you type normally — just your own notes, however you take them. After the meeting, Granola uses AI to merge what you typed with the audio transcript into a structured document.

The result is a hybrid. Your original notes stay. But they're expanded with context from the conversation, cleaned up into proper sentences, and organized into sections.

I tested this across 12 meetings in one week. Status updates. Client calls. Internal planning. The kind of grind that generates 6 hours of audio and zero usable output.

What worked

The hybrid approach is better than either method alone.

My own notes tend to be cryptic. "JD → delay Q3" is what I'd type during a standup. Granola expanded that into "John confirmed the project delay will push deliverables to Q3 (October). Budget reallocation pending finance approval." Every time. Not once did it hallucinate a fake deliverable.

Anna, a product manager at a Series A startup in Berlin, told me she switched for the same reason. "Otter gave me walls of text I never read. Granola gives me my own notes, but complete. I actually look at them the next day."

The local-only approach also means no awkwardness. Nobody asks who joined. No recording banners. Just open the app, take your notes, close it.

Where it falls short

Mac-only. That's the first filter. If you're on Windows or Linux, you can't use it. The web app doesn't exist. The mobile app doesn't exist. You need a Mac for this to work.

The pricing stings too. $40/month or $30/month annual. For a note-taking app. Compared to Otter's free tier, that's a tough sell unless you're in meetings 15+ hours a week.

Search is basic. You can find notes by title or date. Full-text search across meetings works, but it doesn't surface results with any sophistication. If you need to find "that thing Sarah said about the budget three weeks ago," you're scrolling.

The verdict

Granola solves a real problem that the existing tools don't address. The hybrid note-taking model is smarter than bot-in-the-room transcription. And my notes are useful the next morning, which is more than I can say for any other tool I tested.

Buy if: you take meetings on a Mac, you already take your own notes, and you want them to be complete without the overhead of a bot.

Skip if: you want a tool that does everything for you, you're on Windows, or you're under 10 meetings a week. For lighter use, Otter's free tier is fine.

After a week of Granola, I'm not going back. The feeling of opening notes the next day and understanding what I wrote — that's worth the $30/month.
