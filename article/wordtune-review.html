Wordtune Review: It Doesn't Write for You. That's Why It Works.

I thought an AI writing tool would write for you. Wordtune does the opposite. It rewrites what you already wrote. No blank page. No "generate blog post about X." Just your words, made sharper.

I spent a month looking for a writing assistant. Not a generator. I have ChatGPT for that. I wanted something that made my drafts sound like me but better.

First I tried Copy.ai. It was fine for cold emails and social posts. Every output needed rewriting because the tone never matched mine. Jasper had better brand voice controls. $39 a month. I used it for two weeks and realized I was spending more time editing its output than writing from scratch.

I almost gave up. Then a writer I follow on X mentioned Wordtune. "It's not a generator. It's a rewriter. Totally different category." I was skeptical. Every AI tool calls itself unique.

Two weeks later, Wordtune is the only writing tool I kept.

The core trick: rephrase, not generate

You paste a sentence. Wordtune offers 5-10 rewrites in different styles: casual, formal, shorter, longer, expand from bullet points. That's the core feature — rephrase. You write something mediocre. It shows you five better ways to say it.

The first time I tried it, I had a sentence in a client email: "I think this approach would work well for your team." Generic placeholder. Wordtune's suggestions included: "This approach fits your team's workflow." "I'm confident this will work for your team." "Here's why this approach works for your team." None of them were revolutionary. All of them were better than what I wrote.

Wordtune doesn't generate inspiration. It improves execution. If you know what you want to say but can't find the right phrasing, the tool is genuinely useful. If you have no idea what to write, it won't help.

Priya, a content strategist in Chicago who manages newsletters for three B2B companies, told me she uses Wordtune every day. "I write the first draft in 10 minutes. Then I run it through Wordtune. It catches awkward phrasing, tightens wordy sentences, and gives me alternatives I wouldn't think of. My editing time went from 30 minutes to 15."

Casual mode that works

Most AI tools that claim "casual" output still sound professional. Wordtune's casual rewrites sound like someone talking. I tested it on a Slack message draft: "Just a heads up, the deadline moved to Friday." Wordtune Casual: "Heads up. Friday is the new deadline." Shorter. Clearer. More human.

The formal mode is also good. I ran a draft of a project update through it. Wordtune Formal tightened 400 words to 260 without losing meaning. The key change: removing hedging language. It caught "I think" and "we believe" and offered versions without them.

What breaks

Wordtune struggles with creative writing. I tried it on a personal essay intro. It tightened the prose but made it sound like a LinkedIn post. The tool works best on professional communication — emails, reports, social updates, blog posts. Fiction, poetry, anything creative: look elsewhere.

The browser extension is Wordtune's killer feature. It works in Gmail, Google Docs, Slack, LinkedIn, and most text fields. You select text, click the Wordtune icon, and get suggestions inline. No copy-paste. No separate window.

The Spices feature expands a bullet point into a full paragraph. Useful but inconsistent. A bullet of "Q2 revenue grew 12% year over year" expanded into a solid paragraph about growth drivers. Another bullet about "customer churn dropped to 3%" produced a paragraph that repeated the same fact three different ways. When Spices works, it saves time. When it doesn't, you delete and rewrite.

Pricing: Free tier gives 10 rewrites a day, generous enough to test. Plus is $13/month for unlimited rewrites and Spices. Premium at $20/month adds AI-generated responses to emails and messages. The free tier is genuinely useful. I tested for two weeks on the free plan and felt no urgency to upgrade.

Wordtune has one competitor that does something similar: Grammarly. But Grammarly corrects grammar. Wordtune rewrites for clarity and tone. They serve different purposes. Grammarly catches your typos. Wordtune makes your sentences better.

Wordtune is the best AI writing tool for people who already write. Not for people who want AI to write for them.

Get it if you write emails, reports, or social content daily and want your drafts to sound clearer and more natural in half the editing time.

Skip it if you need long-form content generation, creative writing, or a tool that writes from scratch.

After a month of testing generators and rewriters, the tool I kept wasn't the one that could write a blog post from a prompt. It was the one that made my sentences better.
