NotebookLM Review 2025 — Google's AI Notebook Is Way Better Than Expected

May 24, 20268 min read

When Google announced NotebookLM, I assumed it was another AI wrapper — upload a PDF, get a summary, move on. Then I actually used it for a week. The feature that made me stop and pay attention? It turns your notes into a podcast.

I tested NotebookLM against three other AI note-taking tools — Mem, Notion AI, and good old manual note-taking. Here's what surprised me.

What Is NotebookLM?

NotebookLM is Google's AI-powered notebook. You upload documents (PDFs, Google Docs, web links, YouTube transcripts), and it helps you analyze, summarize, and connect ideas across your sources. Unlike ChatGPT or Claude, it's grounded in your uploaded sources — it can't make things up from outside those documents.

Key difference: NotebookLM knows what you've uploaded and refuses to answer from outside that context. This is both its superpower and its limitation.

Test 1: Summarizing a Research Paper

The setup: I uploaded a 25-page PDF about the economic impact of remote work. Asked NotebookLM for a summary.

Result: It returned a 300-word structured summary covering methodology, key findings, limitations, and policy implications. Every claim had a footnote linking back to the exact page in the PDF.

Compared to: I asked Claude the same question about the same PDF. Claude gave a smoother summary but sometimes grouped claims together without distinguishing between the paper's findings and the author's speculation. NotebookLM was more precise because it stayed strictly within the source.

Winner: NotebookLM for precision. Claude for readability.

Real story

Dr. Patel, a postdoc in public health at Johns Hopkins, reviews about 8 papers a week for her systematic review. She tried ChatGPT first but found it sometimes mixed up findings from different papers when she uploaded multiple PDFs. NotebookLM kept each paper separate in its own notebook. "I upload 5 PDFs, ask 'What does each paper say about vaccine hesitancy?' and get 5 separate answers with page numbers. I can verify every claim in under a minute." She estimates it cuts her literature review time by about 40%.

Test 2: The Audio Overview (Podcast Feature)

The setup: I uploaded a dense 15-page consulting report about AI in healthcare. Clicked "Audio Overview." Two minutes later, two AI hosts started discussing the report like a podcast.

The result is genuinely surprising. The AI hosts don't just read the document aloud. They discuss it. One asks a question, the other answers. They disagree occasionally. They say things like "That's a good point, but what about..." It sounds like two smart people who actually read your document and want to talk about it.

Accuracy check: I listened carefully for hallucinations. The conversation stayed faithful to the source material. They didn't invent facts. They did add some conversational filler — "Great question!" — but the substance was accurate.

Winner: NotebookLM, and it's not close. This feature alone is worth trying the tool.

Real story

James, a strategy consultant at a Big 4 firm, uses NotebookLM's Audio Overview before every client meeting. "I upload the industry report, listen to the 10-minute podcast on my drive to the office, and walk in already knowing the key debates in the space. It's like having two analysts brief me in the car." He says he's retained more from the podcast format than from reading the same reports.

Test 3: Cross-Referencing Multiple Sources

The setup: I uploaded 5 different articles about AI regulation — EU AI Act, US executive order, industry responses, academic critiques, and a tech CEO's opinion piece.

Asked: "Where do these sources agree and disagree?"

Result: NotebookLM created a summary table showing points of agreement (need for transparency, risk-based frameworks) and disagreement (enforcement mechanisms, scope of regulation). Each point cited which sources said what.

Compared to: Doing this manually would take 45 minutes of note-taking and comparison. NotebookLM did it in 30 seconds.

Winner: NotebookLM. This is its best use case — synthesizing multiple sources.

Real story

Elena, a legal researcher in New York preparing a memo on AI liability across jurisdictions, uploaded 8 legal documents. She asked NotebookLM "Which jurisdictions impose strict liability vs fault-based liability?" and got a table comparing all 8 sources. "I was dreading this memo. It would have taken me all day to cross-reference everything. NotebookLM gave me a first draft in 30 minutes."

But: The tool missed one important nuance — it categorized a jurisdiction as "strict liability" when the law actually had a "reasonable care" defense that effectively made it fault-based. Always verify the output.

Test 4: Brainstorming & Connecting Ideas

The setup: I uploaded my notes from 4 different books about creativity, habit formation, and productivity. Asked: "What patterns connect these books that the authors themselves might not have noticed?"

Result: NotebookLM identified 3 interesting cross-book themes. One was genuinely insightful — it connected a concept from James Clear's "Atomic Habits" with a cognitive bias discussed in Kahneman's "Thinking, Fast and Slow" in a way I hadn't considered.

Winner: NotebookLM can be genuinely helpful for connecting ideas across sources. But it's not a replacement for your own thinking — it's more like a smart research assistant who points out things you might have missed.

Where NotebookLM Fails

  • Real-time information: NotebookLM can't search the web. It only knows what you uploaded. Ask it "What's the latest news about Google Gemini?" and it will tell you it doesn't have that information.
  • Long conversations: The chat window gets slow after about 30 exchanges. NotebookLM is designed for focused analysis sessions, not extended conversations.
  • Complex instructions: It struggles with multi-step instructions. It handles simpler, more direct queries better.

Winner when it fails: Claude or ChatGPT for general knowledge and complex reasoning. NotebookLM for source-grounded analysis.

NotebookLM is not a replacement for ChatGPT or Claude. It's a specialized tool for one thing — source-grounded analysis — and it does that one thing better than any other AI tool I've tested. If you work with PDFs, research papers, legal documents, or multiple sources that need cross-referencing, it's genuinely useful.

  • Who should use it: Students, researchers, consultants, analysts — anyone who works with documents.
  • Who can skip it: People who rarely work with documents, or need a general-purpose AI assistant.
