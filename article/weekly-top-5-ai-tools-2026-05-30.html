I Tested 5 AI Tools This Week, 2 Earned a Spot in My Workflow

By Alex · Updated May 30, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Hands-on testing over multiple days. Paid plans unless noted. Full methodology on our About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

May 30, 2026 · 7 min read

Last Monday I opened 15 tabs. By Friday, two of them were still open.

That's the pattern I've noticed after months of testing AI tools for this site. A new app promises to change everything. You sign up, you try it, you close the tab. Most don't survive the week. The ones that do, the ones you keep coming back to, those are the ones worth talking about.

This week I tested five tools I'd been hearing about: Replit Agent, Suno v4, HeyGen, VidIQ AI, and Bolt.new. Here's which ones made the cut and which ones I already forgot about.

1. Replit Agent: The One That Actually Delivered

Replit Agent promises to build and deploy apps from a single prompt. I've heard that before. Every AI coding tool says the same thing. But Replit's agent is different in one crucial way: it actually finishes the job.

I asked it to build a simple habit tracker, user auth, a daily check-in form, a streak counter, and a clean dashboard. The agent wrote the code, set up the database, deployed it to a live URL, and handed me the link. All in one session. Total time: 12 minutes.

Was it perfect? No. The UI looked like a developer's first React tutorial. The streak counter had an off-by-one bug I had to fix manually. But here's the thing. I had a working, deployable app 12 minutes after having the idea. That's a fundamentally different experience from every other AI coding tool I've tested.

Marcus, a solo founder from Berlin, told me he's using Replit Agent to prototype features before committing to a full build. "I used to spend two hours scaffolding before writing real logic. Now I spend 12 minutes. The quality floor is higher than I expected."

Verdict: Worth keeping. Not for production apps, but for prototypes and MVPs, nothing else comes close.

2. Suno v4: The Sound Quality Jump Is Real

Suno v4 launched with promises of "studio-quality" AI music. I rolled my eyes. Every AI music update claims better quality. But v4 surprised me.

I generated 10 tracks over the week, pop, lo-fi, synthwave, orchestral. The previous version (v3.5) produced music that sounded like a keyboard demo. v4 sounds like something you could actually use in a video or podcast intro. The instruments separate. The structure holds together. The vocals don't warble into gibberish after 15 seconds.

The catch: Suno still can't do what real producers do. It doesn't understand dynamics, every section is the same volume. It can't take direction like "quieter in the verse, bigger in the chorus." You get what you get. What you get is just better than it used to be.

Anna, a content creator from Toronto who makes daily YouTube videos, tried v4 for her intro music: "I was using paid stock music. Suno v4 gives me something custom that doesn't sound like a template. It's good enough that I canceled my premium subscription."

Verdict: Worth keeping for content creators who need custom background music. Not replacing professional composers.

3. HeyGen: Polished, But For a Narrow Use Case

HeyGen generates AI video avatars that speak your script. The demo on their homepage is impressive, a realistic presenter delivering a sales pitch in perfect sync with the words. I wanted to be impressed.

And I was, at first. The lip-sync is remarkable. The voice cloning captures tone and pacing decently. I made a 60-second product explainer in about 20 minutes.

But then I showed it to a few people. Every single one noticed something was off. "The eyes don't blink naturally." "Her head doesn't move like a real person." "It's uncanny valley — not creepy, but not comfortable either."

For internal training videos or quick social clips? Fine. For customer-facing content where trust matters? Not yet. The technology is impressive. The output still isn't.

Verdict: Useful in specific scenarios (internal comms, social snippets). Not ready for serious brand content.

4. VidIQ AI: The Surprising Workhorse

I didn't expect much from VidIQ. I'd always seen it as a keyword research tool for YouTubers, useful if you run a channel, irrelevant if you don't. But their AI features have quietly become helpful.

The AI title generator didn't just suggest clickbait. It suggested titles that balanced search volume with authenticity. The AI thumbnail analyzer graded my mockups and explained why one design would outperform another, contrast, focal point, text readability. That was more useful than any AI content generator I've tested.

Tom, who runs a small tech education channel, has been using VidIQ for six months: "The AI daily idea generator gives me 10 topic suggestions based on what's trending in my niche. About 3 of them are actually good. That's way better than staring at a blank page."

Verdict: Worth keeping if you create any video content. The AI features are practical, not gimmicky.

5. Bolt.new: Fast, But Not Sticky

Bolt.new (by StackBlitz) competes directly with Replit Agent. Same promise: describe an app, get a deployed app. So I gave it the same test, a habit tracker with auth, check-ins, and streaks.

Bolt.new was faster at the initial generation. The UI looked better out of the box. It handled the auth flow more cleanly. But then I tried to make changes. Editing existing functionality was frustrating, small tweaks sometimes triggered full regenerations. The state management felt fragile.

Replit Agent lets you fix things one piece at a time. Bolt.new wants to regenerate everything. For quick prototypes, that's fine. For iterating on a real project, it's a dealbreaker.

Verdict: Impressive first impression. But Replit Agent wins for anyone who needs to iterate.

The Keepers vs The Rest

Two tools are staying in my workflow: Replit Agent for prototyping and VidIQ AI for content planning. They solve real problems without adding friction.

Suno v4 is close. I'll keep it installed for quick audio needs, but I'm not building a workflow around it yet. HeyGen and Bolt.new are talented tools that need more iteration before they're indispensable.

The pattern I keep seeing: the tools that survive are the ones that integrate into existing habits, not ones that ask you to change how you work. Replit Agent fits between having an idea and opening your editor. VidIQ fits between planning content and publishing it. They don't replace your process, they improve the step that was already slowest.

Next week, I'm testing some newer entrants in the AI video space. If the pattern holds, one of them will stick.
