I Built an Affiliate Site With AI. It Made $84 in Month One.

By Alex · Updated June 1, 2026 · AI Pickz

June 1, 2026 6 min read

$84 doesn't sound like much. That's a dinner for two at a mid-range restaurant. A pair of sneakers on sale. But $84 is also infinity percent more than the last affiliate site I built, which made exactly $0 before I abandoned it six weeks in.

I'd always written off AI-generated content for affiliate marketing. Seemed like a race to the bottom — everyone pumping out the same GPT-written lists of "best coffee makers" with the same filler paragraphs. I wasn't wrong about that. What I was wrong about was thinking AI can't help at all.

The skeptic's setup

I picked a niche I know something about: home office ergonomics. Standing desks, monitor arms, ergonomic chairs. Small enough that I could write from experience, big enough that products have decent affiliate commissions (desk companies run 5-8% on $300-800 items).

I built a bare-bones site on a $12 domain. Five article pages, one comparison table, a "best of" roundup. No fancy design. No email list. Just content.

For research, I used Perplexity to find the latest ergonomic studies and product specs. For drafting, I used Claude to turn my bullet points into first-pass prose. I edited everything manually — cutting fluff, adding my own experience, restructuring paragraphs. The final output was maybe 40% AI-written, 60% rewritten.

What surprised me

Two things worked better than I expected.

Keyword research. I threw broad topics into Perplexity and asked for long-tail variations. Things like "best standing desk for tall people under $500" or "monitor arm for thick desk." These are specific searches real buyers use. I wouldn't have thought of half of them on my own.

Comparison tables. Writing "Product A vs Product B" content is mechanical work. I listed the specs, Claude formatted them into a table, and I adjusted the rankings based on my own product knowledge. This was the article that drove most of the traffic.

Where AI fell apart

The articles I let AI write mostly, where I just prompted and published, got zero traffic. Zero impressions in Google Search Console. It wasn't a penalty. The content was just generic. Every "best standing desk" article on the internet says the same things. AI produces a good average, but average doesn't win in SEO.

An article I rewrote heavily from scratch, the standing desk comparison, started getting clicks by week three.

Common scenario

Jake, a product manager in Austin, runs a small affiliate site about mechanical keyboards. He uses Claude for outlines and research summaries only. "The first time I let AI write a full article, it told people to 'delve into the world of mechanical switches.' I deleted the whole thing."

He writes the reviews himself, drawing from keyboards he's owned and tested. His site makes around $400/month, mostly from Amazon affiliate links. He says the AI's best use is competitive analysis — feeding it 10 competitor articles and asking for the gaps.

The honest verdict

AI is good for the research phase. It's good for formatting comparisons. It's bad at writing content that ranks, because ranking content needs something AI doesn't have: a point of view.

I'd tell a friend to try this approach: use AI for the grunt work (keyword research, outline structure, data formatting), but write the recommendations yourself. The $84 came from articles where I did most of the thinking. The zero-traffic articles were the ones I outsourced.

The site is still up, by the way. I add a new article every couple of weeks. If I can get it to $200/month by summer, I might call it a real side hustle instead of just an experiment.

Should you try this?

Try it if: you already know a niche and need help with research and formatting. AI can save you hours on the mechanical parts.

Skip if: you're hoping AI can write content that ranks on its own. It won't. The value is in your opinion, not the AI's average.
