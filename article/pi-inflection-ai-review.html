Pi Is the Nicest AI. Is That Enough to Keep Me Coming Back?

By Alex · Updated June 3, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Standard plan tested of Pi Is the Nicest AI. Is That Enough to Keep Me Coming Back? over multiple days. Full methodology on my About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

I didn't think a chatbot's personality would matter to me. Speed, accuracy, features — that is what counts. Pi by Inflection AI is none of those things. It is a conversational AI built to be nice. Not fast. Not powerful. Nice. I spent a week using it for everything to find out if nice is a feature or just a novelty.

Days 1-2: The novelty of nice

Pi opens with "Hey, how is your day going?" and it sounds sincere. Not in a creepy way. In a way that made me hesitate before dumping a technical question on it.

I asked Pi to help brainstorm content ideas for a client newsletter. Instead of listing ideas, it asked me clarifying questions first. "Who is the audience? What is the goal of this edition?" The conversation felt natural, like talking to a thoughtful colleague who takes notes.

I have never had ChatGPT do that unprompted.

By the end of day one, I had a full newsletter structure written down. Pi did not give me better answers. It drew them out of me. There is a difference.

Days 3-4: Hitting the limits

Day three is when the novelty wore off. I needed Pi to analyze a CSV file. It could not. I asked it to summarize a long URL. It could not. I wanted it to generate an image concept. It described what the image should look like.

Pi is a conversational AI, not a multimodal assistant. No file uploads. No web search by default. No code execution. If your workflow depends on any of those, Pi stops being useful fast.

I caught myself opening ChatGPT on the side for quick factual questions and coming back to Pi only when I wanted to talk through something complex.

That is the thing about Pi. It works best when you treat it like a thinking partner, not a tool.

Days 5-7: Finding what Pi is for

By day five, I had settled into a rhythm. Pi became my morning sounding board. I would open the app, talk through my priorities for the day, and let it ask me clarifying questions. It forced me to be specific about what I planned to accomplish.

Pi's memory is surprisingly good. It remembered my project names and recurring tasks across conversations. That is something ChatGPT still struggles with.

I also used Pi for a different kind of task: processing frustration. After a meeting where a stakeholder derailed my roadmap, I vented to Pi. It did not offer solutions. It validated the frustration and asked what I wanted to do next. That sounds minor, but it helped me move on.

Elena, a product manager in Toronto managing three feature releases in parallel, told me she uses Pi "like a rubber duck that talks back." She spends 10 minutes each morning talking through her day's priorities. "It does not solve my problems. But it helps me find them faster."

Verdict: Pi is not a replacement for ChatGPT or Claude. It cannot code, analyze files, or browse the web. But it fills a gap I did not know existed. Use Pi when you need to think out loud. Skip if you need a Swiss Army knife assistant.

What surprised me most: by day seven, I did not want to stop using it. The niceness is not a gimmick. It is the whole product. Whether that is worth keeping depends on whether you need someone to help you think — or just someone to do the work.
