I'm Tired of AI Tools That Promise Everything. These 4 Actually Delivered.

By Alex · Updated June 1, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Hands-on testing over multiple days. Paid plans unless noted. Full methodology on our About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

June 1, 2026 · 7 min read

I have over 40 accounts with AI tools I'll never open again. Some were free trials I forgot to cancel. Others were paid subscriptions I kept meaning to kill. They pile up.

Every new launch promises the same things: 10x productivity, zero learning curve, magic. After you've seen 50 of these landing pages, you stop believing the copy. You start asking a different question: does this tool solve something I do, or is it creating a problem so it can sell me the solution?

I tested 5 tools this week. One didn't survive day one. The other four earned a bookmark folder. Here's why.

1. Claude Code: The terminal tool I didn't know I needed

I didn't want to like Claude Code. A terminal-based coding agent sounded like over-engineering for problems I didn't have. I already use Copilot for autocomplete and Cursor for heavier edits. Why would I type claude in my terminal?

Then I gave it a real task. I had a Python script that parsed CSV files, cleaned data, and generated reports. The code worked but was held together with duct tape. One function was 300 lines. Error handling was a prayer.

I ran claude refactor this and described what I wanted. It didn't just suggest changes. It read the whole file, asked clarifying questions about edge cases, rewrote the module into 6 clean functions, added type hints and error handling, and showed me the diff. I reviewed it in 4 minutes. I accepted 3 minutes later.

The difference from Copilot or Cursor is subtle. Those tools complete your thought. Claude Code completes the task. It thinks about architecture, not just the next line.

James, a backend engineer at a fintech startup in Austin, told me he's using Claude Code for migrations. "I had to refactor 12 API endpoints. Copilot would have helped me write each endpoint. Claude Code looked at all 12 and asked if I wanted to extract a shared middleware. That's a different level of understanding."

Verdict: Worth keeping. Not for beginners who need handholding, but for experienced developers doing complex refactors or multi-file changes, it's different from every other coding AI.

2. Suno v4: Still the best AI music, with one big caveat

I wrote about Suno v3.5 a few months ago and called it "a fun toy." v4 is not a toy. The sound quality jump from 3.5 to 4 is larger than any other AI music update I've seen. Instruments separate. Vocals don't drift into static. The structure holds.

I generated 8 tracks: a lo-fi study beat, a synthwave track, a folk song with lyrics I wrote, a commercial jingle. The lo-fi track could pass for something off a Chillhop playlist. The jingle made my wife laugh when I played it in the kitchen. "That's not bad," she said. High praise from someone who tolerates my experiments.

The caveat: Suno locks you in. You cannot edit the song after generation. Want a longer intro? Remake the whole track. Want different lyrics in the bridge? Start over. The generation process is a black box. You seed, it creates. If you don't like what you get, you roll the dice again.

For quick background music or inspiration, that's fine. For anything where you need control over arrangement or duration, you'll hit a wall.

Verdict: Worth keeping for content creators who need quick, custom background music. Not for anyone who needs control over structure or arrangement.

3. Kling AI: Video generation that almost feels like a camera

Kling 1.6 launched with a claim that caught my attention: "cinematic quality at 1080p." Most AI video looks like a dream sequence — blurry, morphing, unstable. I expected more of the same.

The first clip I generated changed my mind. A prompt about a woman walking through a rainy street at night. Kling rendered it with raindrops that hit the pavement and splash. The streetlights cast actual reflections on the wet ground. The woman's coat moved naturally with each step.

I watched it 5 times looking for artifacts. There were some. The far background flickered. The woman's face changed subtly between frames. But the overall quality was closer to real video than anything I've seen from Runway or Pika.

The catch: generation takes 5-10 minutes per clip. Making it usable requires patience. And Kling's prompt adherence is inconsistent. I asked for a "sunset beach scene" and got something that looked like dawn. I specified "orange sky" and got gray.

Verdict: Worth keeping for visual creators who can work around the quirks. The best AI video generator for realism in June 2026 — but "best" still comes with asterisks.

4. Beautiful.ai: Finally, an AI slides tool that doesn't make ugly decks

I've tried Gamma, Tome, and Decktopus. They all produce decent slides, but they all suffer from the same problem: the AI controls the layout, which means you spend more time fighting the templates than writing content.

Beautiful.ai takes a different approach. Instead of generating the whole deck from one prompt, it acts like a design assistant. You drag in content, images, bullet lists. The AI arranges them intelligently. If you move something, everything else adjusts automatically.

I built a 10-slide pitch deck for a fictional SaaS product. The first version took 20 minutes. I spent another 15 minutes tweaking colors and fonts. The result looked better than decks I've seen from agencies charging $2,000.

The limitation: you're locked into their design system. You can customize colors and fonts, but the slide layouts are templates. If you want something structurally unique, you'll fight the tool. And Beautiful.ai is expensive at $12/month for Pro compared to Google Slides (free) with an AI add-on.

But if your goal is a professional deck fast, the trade-off is worth it.

Verdict: Worth keeping for anyone who regularly builds presentations. The design assistant model beats fully-automated generators for actual control.

5. HeyGen: Impressive demo, hard to use in practice

I saved the most disappointing for last. HeyGen's AI avatars look great in their marketing. A realistic presenter, lip-sync perfect, gestures natural. The demo page is convincing.

In practice, I hit limits fast.

First attempt: a 90-second product explainer. I uploaded my script, picked an avatar, clicked generate. The output was good for the first 30 seconds. Then the avatar's head stopped moving. The eyes lost focus. The voice delivery flattened out, word after word at the same pace with the same emphasis.

I tried a shorter 30-second version. Better, but the uncanny valley was still there. Something about the skin texture, the lack of micro-expressions. A friend watched it and said, "Is that a real person? I can't tell. But I feel like I should be able to tell."

For internal training videos that nobody will scrutinize, HeyGen works. For customer-facing content, the risk is real. A visitor who senses something is off will distrust everything you say.

Verdict: Not keeping. Impressive technology that isn't production-ready for serious brand content. Check back in a year.

5 tools. 4 bookmarks. 1 uninstall. That's a better ratio than most weeks. The ones that survived had one thing in common: they respected my time by doing work I didn't want to do, not work I was already doing.
