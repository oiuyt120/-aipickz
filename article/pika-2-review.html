I Made 80 AI Videos in 7 Days With Pika 2.0. Three Were Worth Keeping.

By Alex · Updated June 2, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Standard plan tested of I Made 80 AI Videos in 7 Days With Pika 2.0. Three Were Worth Keeping. over multiple days. Full methodology on my About page.

I generated 80 videos in seven days. 12 were usable. 3 were good. The other 65 ranged from unsettling to a cat having a seizure.

That sounds like a terrible hit rate. But the 3 that worked? I almost could not tell they were AI-generated. One of them I showed to a friend and asked "guess how this was made." She said a stock footage site. That is the moment Pika 2.0 stopped being a toy.

I wanted to run a clean experiment. Seven days. Pika 2.0 only. No Runway, no Kling, no Luma. I tracked what worked, what broke, and when I should have switched tools.

Day 1: The text prompt wall

I started with what everyone tries: text to video. "A woman in a red dress walks through a rainy city street at night." Classic test prompt. Almost every AI video creator has tried this one.

Pika returned a 5-second clip. The lighting was good. The rain looked real. The woman walked maybe four steps. Then her face melted into something that belonged in a horror movie.

I tried 12 more text prompts that day. Same pattern: beautiful first 2 seconds, then the character falls apart. Pika 2.0 handles wide shots better than close-ups. Landscapes, buildings, abstract scenes. Anything with a human face in frame longer than 3 seconds risks turning into a glitch.

Day 1 score: 0 keepers out of 13 tries. I went to bed annoyed.

Day 3: Image to video changes everything

The breakthrough came when I stopped typing descriptions and started uploading photos. Pika 2.0 has a "reference image" feature. You upload a still image and the model animates it. This exists in other tools too, but Pika handles motion better than most.

I uploaded a shot from my phone of a bridge at sunset. Generic photo. Pika added slow camera motion: a gentle pan upward while clouds drifted and water reflected warm light. The output was 4 seconds of footage that looked like a establishing shot from a documentary. No faces to corrupt. No limbs to misplace. Just a beautiful moving image.

That clip made the "stock footage" comment from my friend.

I also tried it on product shots. A photo of a minimalist desk lamp. Pika added a slow zoom with subtle light flicker. The lamp stayed recognizable the whole time. For ecommerce or social media teasers, this alone is worth the subscription.

Day 5: Pushing the limits

I tested the lip sync feature. Upload a face photo and an audio clip. Pika generates a video of the face speaking your words. It works about 40% of the time. The mouth moves. The timing is rough but close enough for a joke post.

The other 60% is uncanny valley territory. Mouth shapes that do not match vowels. The head staying perfectly still while the jaw moves independently. Pika 2.0 is not ready for talking head videos.

Style transfer worked better than I expected. I uploaded a photo of a street in my neighborhood in the middle of winter. Prompt: "make it look like a Miyazaki film." Pika added soft lighting, exaggerated clouds, and a slight color shift toward green and teal. The clip felt more like a memory than a video. I cannot explain it technically, but the mood was right.

Then I tried the same prompt on a photo of a parking lot. Pika made it look like a suburban apocalypse. Trees were wrong. Shadows stretched sideways. The AI had no reference for "Miyazaki parking lot" and guessed badly. Context matters.

Aisha, a social media manager in Toronto who runs Instagram Reels for a boutique hotel chain, told me she uses Pika for "atmosphere clips." She uploads photos of rooms and lets Pika add slow motion. "I post 5 Reels a week. Pika handles the B-roll. I used to pay $200 a month for stock footage. This replaces most of it." But she also said she tried using it for a client's face-heavy ad and abandoned it after 15 renders. "The eyes were never right."

Day 7: What survived

At the end of the week, I had 3 keepers out of 80 renders. Two were landscape animations from my own photos. One was a product shot. Zero were text-to-video. Zero involved human faces.

That is the honest summary of Pika 2.0 right now. If you need scenic establishing shots, product animations, or atmospheric B-roll, it is useful. If you need a character walking down a street looking human for more than 3 seconds, use Runway.

Pricing matters. Pika 2.0 starts at $10/month for 180 credits. Each render costs 1-5 credits depending on resolution and length. The $10 tier gives about 50-100 videos. Runway's comparable plan is $15/month. For B-roll, Pika wins on price. For anything with people, Runway wins on quality.

The mobile app was a nice surprise. Pika's iOS app let me generate from my phone during a commute. The interface is minimal and works fine for quick tests. I made one of my 3 keepers on the train.

Pika 2.0 wins at one thing: turning still photos into moving atmosphere. That is a bigger use case than it sounds like. Social media content creators, indie filmmakers on a budget, ecommerce teams — the people I'd recommend it to most are not looking for character animation. They need background clips that look like they hired a camera crew.

Buy if you need atmospheric video from photos — establishing shots, product animations, or social media B-roll.

Skip if you need talking heads, character-driven narratives, or anything with a human face in focus for more than 3 seconds.

I kept Pika installed after the trial. But I still open Runway when a project demands a person walking down a street without disintegrating. The two tools sit in different lanes. My week proved which lane Pika owns.
