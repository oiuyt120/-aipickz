Anyword Review: The AI That Predicts Which Headline Will Actually Work

June 4, 2026 6 min read Writing & Content

I had a landing page that was fine. Not great. Fine. The kind of page that gets traffic but converts at a rate best described as "technically above zero."

I rewrote the headline seven times. Each version felt better. Each version changed nothing.

That's when I started looking for AI tools that do more than generate text. I wanted something that could tell me if what I wrote would work before I spent money on traffic. Not suggestions. Prediction.

Anyword is the closest I have found to that.

How I got here

Most AI writing tools work the same way. You type a prompt, it generates options, you pick one. The generated copy can sound great and still convert like garbage. There is no feedback loop until you pay for visitors.

I went through weeks of that cycle. Generate 20 headline options. Pick the one that sounded best. Run ads. Hope. Results were random.

Anyword takes a different approach. You write or generate copy, then it scores each version against a model trained on real ad and landing page data. The score tells you how likely someone is to engage before you spend a cent.

The prediction engine is real

I tested this on a landing page I had been optimizing manually. I wrote 10 headline variations and ran them through Anyword's predictive score.

The highest-scoring headline predicted a 67% engagement rate. I ran it through Google Ads for three days at $20/day. The actual rate came back at 62%.

Close enough to be useful.

The lowest-scoring headline predicted 31%. Actual: 28%.

Anyword was not wrong about which headline would perform. It was off by a few points. But the ranking was consistent across every test.

The feature that made me keep it

Anyword has a target audience setting. You specify demographics, interests, and intent. The model adjusts the copy score based on who you are writing for.

I wrote a Facebook ad for a project management tool. General score: 72. After setting the target to "freelancers who work remotely," the same ad scored 88. The model picked up phrasing that works better for that group and adjusted accordingly.

Tom, a marketing manager at a B2B SaaS startup in Austin, told me he uses this to test copy before spending money. "Instead of running eight variations at $50 each, I test 30 in Anyword for free and launch only the top three. My cost per acquisition dropped 35% in the first month."

Where it falls short

The scoring works best for short copy. Landing page headlines, ad copy, email subject lines, social posts. Precise, short, high-stakes text.

Long-form is a different story. I tested a 500-word sales page and the score barely moved between three very different versions. The model doesn't seem to have enough signal density for longer text.

One catch: Anyword does not generate great copy from scratch. The generation tools are fine. Not bad. Not remarkable. Think of Anyword as a scoring layer on top of copy you or another AI wrote. The generation is adequate. The scoring is why you subscribe.

The verdict

Get it if:

You spend money on ads. Anyword pays for itself in reduced testing. At $49/month for the Starter plan, you need to save one bad ad campaign to break even.

Skip it if:

You only write blog posts or long-form content. The scoring loses accuracy over longer text, and the generation tools are not worth the price on their own.

Otherwise, it is the only AI copywriting tool I have used where the output includes a prediction, not just a suggestion. That alone makes it worth a month to test.
