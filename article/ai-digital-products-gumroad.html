I Didn't Think Anyone Would Pay for AI-Generated Templates. Then I Got My First Sale

By Alex · Updated May 29, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Personal experiment: built and sold digital templates over 4 weeks. Full methodology on my About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

May 29, 2026 · 7 min read

I was convinced the digital products market was a bubble waiting to pop.

Every week on Twitter someone announces they made $10,000 selling Notion templates. On TikTok, creators flash their Gumroad revenue screenshots like lottery tickets. The internet is drowning in "10 Aesthetic Planners" and "Ultimate Life OS Dashboards." And I figured, if everyone's selling templates, nobody's buying them, right?

I was wrong. But admitting that might be the most useful thing I learned all month.

Here's what happened when a skeptic tried to build a digital products store with AI, and why I still haven't fully changed my mind.

What I Thought Would Happen

My assumption was straightforward: the market is oversaturated, differentiation is impossible, and the only people making money are the ones selling courses about selling templates. I went into this expecting to confirm my bias.

I picked three product types to test: Notion templates for freelancers, printable budget planners, and social media content calendars. I used Notion AI to draft the template ideas and Canva Magic Studio to design the printable versions. Total time investment: about six hours spread across a weekend. Total cost: zero additional dollars (I already had both subscriptions).

The products looked decent. Notion AI suggested useful categories for a freelance client tracker. Canva's templates for budget planners were well-designed out of the box. I uploaded everything to Gumroad, set prices between $5 and $15, and waited.

That first week? Four views. Zero sales.

What Changed My Mind

Week two, something unexpected happened. Someone on a freelancing subreddit asked for a simple project tracker template. I linked my Notion template, not as a sales pitch, just as a "here's what I use." Five people downloaded it that day. Two of them paid the $7 price. One sent a message: "This is exactly what I needed. Thank you."

That message changed something. Not because it was heartwarming, because it revealed what I'd been missing. The AI didn't make my template better than anyone else's. But it removed the friction of creating it from scratch, which meant I could make something good enough for a specific niche that wasn't being served well.

Anna, a freelancer from Melbourne who sells digital planners on Etsy, told me: "I use Canva's AI for my base designs, but I spend most of my time on the layout and usability. The AI gives me a starting point that would take me an hour to build. I fix the details in 20 minutes. That's the whole trick."

That clicked for me. The value wasn't in the AI generation. It was in the editing. The AI could produce a functional Notion database or a decent-looking planner page. But the useful products required a human to look at it and think "this flow makes no sense, let me restructure it."

Week two total: 7 sales, $39 in revenue. Not life-changing. But it broke my hypothesis.

The Ceiling I Hit

By week three, I'd listed 12 products. Sales trickled in, one or two a day. Gumroad's discoverability is terrible (you basically need external traffic). Organic growth was stalled.

And here's the part I haven't resolved: my products were getting good reviews but nobody was sharing them. Nobody was coming back for a second purchase. The lifetime value of a digital product customer, if you're an anonymous seller with no brand, is essentially one transaction.

I tried Gumroad's affiliate program, you offer creators 30% commission to promote your products. Two people signed up. Neither made a sale. The products just weren't remarkable enough to motivate anyone to push them.

Tom, a creator who earned $3,200 from Gumroad last year, explained: "The AI template market is huge, but the winners have a following first. They use the template as a lead magnet, not a product. The money comes from the community, the newsletter, the coaching calls, not the $7 template itself."

That's the real bottleneck. AI can help you create a product. It cannot help you build an audience.

What I Still Won't Use Digital Products For

Full-time income. That's the short answer.

After a month, I made $84 from digital products. Subtract the Gumroad fees (about 9% per transaction), and I netted roughly $76. That's not a side hustle. That's a weekend project that covers a dinner.

Could I scale it? Probably. If I built an audience on Twitter, started a newsletter, created high-ticket products (courses, templates bundles, Notion certification prep), and treated it like a real business instead of an experiment. But that's 90% audience-building and 10% product creation. AI only helps with the easy 10%.

I'll keep the store running. I might even add more products when I have a concrete idea. But I'm not quitting my day job, and I wouldn't advise anyone else to either, unless you already have an audience and need help creating faster.

The Verdict

Try AI for digital products if: you already have a small audience and want to create lead magnets or low-ticket offers without spending weeks on design. Notion AI and Canva can cut your creation time by 70%.

Skip if: you're hoping to build passive income from scratch with no following. The products don't sell themselves. The AI didn't fix distribution, it just made production faster. Distribution is still the hard part, and no AI tool has cracked that yet.

$76 in a month taught me that AI can make you a creator. It cannot make you a business.
