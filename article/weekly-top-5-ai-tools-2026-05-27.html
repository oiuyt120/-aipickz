This Week's Best AI Tools — I Found 2 That Actually Surprised Me

By Alex · Updated May 27, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Hands-on testing over multiple days. Paid plans unless noted. Full methodology on our About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

May 27, 20266 min read

Every week I test a handful of AI tools. Most are fine. Some are forgettable. A rare few make me change how I work.

This week I went in skeptical. The AI landscape in late May 2026 is crowded, every week brings another "game-changing" launch, another me-too wrapper, another promise that fizzles by Friday. I didn't expect much.

Two of these five surprised me. One made me laugh (in a good way). Here's the real breakdown.

1. Claude Code. The CLI Agent That Actually Works

I didn't believe the hype. Another AI coding tool? I've tested Cursor, Copilot, Windsurf, and Cline this month alone. What could Anthropic possibly add?

Turns out: a lot. Claude Code runs entirely in your terminal. No VS Code extension, no IDE plugin. You type claude "refactor this module to use async/await" and it reads your files, understands your project structure, and makes the changes, then explains what it did.

I pointed it at a messy Express.js API I'd been meaning to clean up. It added error handling middleware, extracted route handlers into separate files, and wrote unit tests for the new endpoints. All in one session. Did it perfectly? No, one test had a wrong import path. But the structure was 80% right, and fixing that import took 10 seconds.

Why it surprised me: It doesn't just complete code. It understands intent. I asked it to "make this endpoint more secure" and it added rate limiting, input validation, and a JWT check, things Copilot never suggests unprompted.

Best for: Refactoring, code review, multi-step changes across files

Price: Usage included with Claude Pro ($20/month) or via API

2. Supermaven. The Fastest Autocomplete I've Used

I'd heard the claims. "Supermaven predicts your next edit before you finish typing." I rolled my eyes. Then I installed it.

It's not an exaggeration. Supermaven's autocomplete fires instantly. I mean instantly. Copilot takes about 400ms to suggest a completion. Cursor is around 200ms. Supermaven is under 50ms. The difference is noticeable: your cursor never catches up. The suggestion is already there, waiting.

I used it for three days straight, writing Python, TypeScript, and Go. The short completions (single lines, variable names) are almost always right. The long completions (multi-line blocks) are hit-or-miss, about 60% usable, but at this speed, dismissing a bad suggestion costs nothing.

Tom, a backend engineer in Austin, told me: "I installed it Monday morning. By Tuesday, I couldn't go back. It's not that the code is better — it's that I'm not waiting. That break in flow was costing me way more than I realized."

Best for: Daily coding in Python, TypeScript, Go; anyone who values typing speed

Price: Free (limited), $10/month (Pro)

3. Murf AI. Voiceovers That Don't Sound Like a GPS

I needed a voiceover for a product demo. Normally I'd record myself, but I was losing my voice. A friend suggested Murf AI, a text-to-speech tool aimed at businesses and content creators.

First impression: the voice library is massive. 120+ voices across 20 languages. A lot of them are still clearly AI, that slight robotic sheen on sustained vowels. But about 20 voices are hard to distinguish from a human recording.

I picked an American male voice (named "Chris"), dropped in my script, and adjusted the pacing. The result was good enough that I used it. Would a trained voice actor sound better? Yes. But the actor costs $300 and takes 3 days. Murf cost me $0 and took 15 minutes.

The real win: Murf lets you add emphasis to specific words, adjust pitch on individual sentences, and insert natural pauses. Most voice AI tools just read the words. Murf lets you direct the performance.

Best for: Product demos, e-learning content, YouTube videos, internal training

Price: $19/month (Basic), $49/month (Pro)

4. Poe AI. The Aggregator for People Who Can't Pick One Model

Poe isn't new. Quora launched it in 2023. But it's gotten quietly excellent. If you're the kind of person who switches between ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Grok based on the task (I am), Poe saves you three browser tabs and two subscriptions.

Poe gives you all the major models in one interface. Claude for writing. ChatGPT for brainstorming. Gemini for research. Grok for... Grok things. You pay one subscription ($19.99/month) and get a monthly credit to use across all models.

Sarah, a freelance writer in Berlin, told me she uses Poe because "I don't want to think about which model to use. I want to think about what I'm writing. Poe lets me try all of them without guilt."

One standout feature: Poe lets you create custom bots. You pick the base model, write a system prompt, and share a link. I made a "Legal Reviewer" bot using Claude Sonnet that checks my freelance contracts. Takes 2 minutes to set up. Saves me a lawyer consult fee every time.

Best for: People who use 2+ AI models regularly, anyone who hates managing multiple subscriptions

Price: $19.99/month (unlimited access to all models)

5. Perplexity Spaces. Research That Actually Remembers

Perplexity has been my default search engine for three months. Their latest feature, Spaces, is what finally made me cancel my Google search bookmark.

Spaces are persistent research folders. You create a Space for a project, say, "Planning a trip to Japan" or "Researching CRM tools" — and every search, every follow-up question, every saved source lives in that Space. When you come back a week later, Perplexity remembers what you were doing. It knows what you've already found, so it doesn't repeat itself.

I created a Space for this very article, researching the tools above. Every search for "Supermaven pricing" and "Claude Code CLI setup" is in one place. When I asked "what did I find about pricing?", Perplexity pulled from my previous searches in that Space. No re-Googling. That's the kind of feature you don't appreciate until you use it for a week.

Best for: Long-running research projects, trip planning, product comparisons, academic work

Price: Free (limited), $20/month (Pro)

The One I'm Still Using

I was ready to dismiss all five and move on. But Claude Code and Perplexity Spaces are still on my taskbar at the end of the week. Claude Code changed my terminal workflow. Perplexity Spaces changed my research workflow. That's two wins out of five, a great week by my standards.

Supermaven is fast but not essential. Murf AI is useful if you make video content regularly. Poe is a convenience play, worth it if you're juggling multiple subscriptions, but not transformative for single-model users.
