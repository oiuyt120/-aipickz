Adobe Firefly Review 2025 — Adobe's AI Image Generator Tested Head-to-Head

May 26, 20267 min read

Adobe has been in the creative software game for four decades. So when they launched Firefly, their AI image generator, everyone expected something polished. But polished doesn't always mean best. I spent two weeks testing Firefly across its core features, text-to-image, Generative Fill, text effects, and 3D-to-image, and put it head-to-head with Midjourney and DALL-E. Here's what I found.

What Is Adobe Firefly?

Firefly is Adobe's family of generative AI models, built directly into the Creative Cloud ecosystem. It launched in early 2023 and has since expanded across Photoshop (Generative Fill), Illustrator (vector recolor), and a standalone web app at firefly.adobe.com.

The big differentiator: commercial safety. Adobe trained Firefly on licensed content (Adobe Stock and public domain works) and offers full indemnification for commercial use. If you're a brand or agency worried about copyright lawsuits, and you should be, given the legal uncertainty around Midjourney and Stable Diffusion. Firefly is the only major AI image generator that doesn't leave you exposed.

Pricing: Firefly is included with Creative Cloud subscriptions (Photoshop, Illustrator, etc.). The standalone web app gives you 25 free generative credits per month. Paid plans start at $4.99/month for 100 credits.

Test 1: Text-to-Image. Firefly Image 3 Model

Firefly's latest Image 3 model handles a wide range of prompts. I tested: "A minimalist Scandinavian living room with natural wood and white walls, sunlight streaming through large windows," and "An astronaut riding a neon-colored dragon through a futuristic city, cyberpunk style."

The minimalist room was nearly perfect. Clean lines, warm lighting, realistic wood grain. The AI understood "Scandinavian" as a design aesthetic, not just a location. The astronaut and dragon? Decent composition, but the detail work lagged behind Midjourney. The dragon's scales looked muddled at full resolution, and the neon glow didn't pop the way it would in Midjourney.

Verdict: Firefly excels at photorealistic scenes, product photography, interior design, lifestyle imagery. It struggles with fantastical or highly stylized prompts where Midjourney shines. If your work is commercial product visualization, Firefly is your best bet. For concept art and creative exploration, Midjourney still wins.

Test 2: Generative Fill in Photoshop

This is Firefly's killer feature. Open any image in Photoshop, select an area, and type what you want to add or remove. I tested it on a portrait photo where I wanted to extend the background, adding a bookshelf, a window, and a potted plant to the empty space behind the subject.

The results were seamless. The bookshelf matched the photo's lighting and perspective. The window cast realistic shadows. The potted plant had proper depth. I spent five minutes on what would normally take a professional retoucher an hour.

I also tested object removal: deleting a tourist from a travel photo. Firefly filled the gap perfectly, reconstructing the background wall and ground without any visible seams. Better than Photoshop's old Content-Aware Fill by a mile.

Caveat: Generative Fill is only as good as your selection. Make a sloppy selection, and the AI fills the sloppy parts, giving you weird artifacts at the edges. And people with complex anatomy (hands, crossed arms) still trip it up, fingers merge, limbs get confused.

Test 3: Text Effects and Typography

Firefly's Text Effects feature lets you apply textures and styles to text using AI prompts. I typed "SUMMER SALE" and prompted: "Gold metallic texture with sparkling highlights, premium feel." The result was a high-end-looking text treatment that would have taken 30 minutes in Illustrator.

I tried "neon sign on brick wall" and "wood carving effect" — both produced convincing, print-ready results. For social media managers and small business owners who need quick graphics, this feature alone is worth the $4.99/month plan. It saves hours of manual text styling.

Test 4: 3D-to-Image

This is a unique Firefly feature. You upload a rough 3D model (or use one of Adobe's presets), pose it, and Firefly generates a fully rendered, styled image from your 3D composition. I placed a simple block character model on a sphere and prompted: "A robot exploring an alien planet, cinematic lighting."

The result was impressive, proper lighting, atmospheric fog, and dramatic shadows that matched the 3D geometry. It's not as good as manual 3D rendering with Blender or Cinema 4D, but for rapid prototyping and concept visualization, it's incredibly fast. Game designers and previs artists will find this useful for iterating on scene compositions.

Real Stories

Sarah, an e-commerce product photographer in Portland, switched to Firefly for her catalog work. "I shoot hundreds of products a month. With Generative Fill, I can change backgrounds, add props, and extend scenes without reshooting. One shot becomes ten variations. My clients love the variety, and I've cut my lighting setup time by 60%. The downside? I still need to shoot the actual product. Firefly changes the product details if I try to generate from scratch. I use it as an editor, not a creator."

Tom, a graphic designer at a marketing agency in Chicago, uses Firefly for client presentations. "The commercial indemnification is non-negotiable for us. Our legal team won't touch Midjourney. Firefly means I can generate mockups for Coca-Cola or Nike without worrying about copyright claims. But I still find myself frustrated by the prompt limits. Twenty-five credits a month is nothing. I burn through those in a morning. The $4.99 plan is bare minimum for actual work."

The Downsides

  • Credit system is too stingy: 25 free credits per month is a joke. Even the $4.99 plan (100 credits) runs out fast with heavy use
  • Less creative freedom: Firefly is conservative by design. It won't generate violent, scary, or adult content. Useful for brands, frustrating for artists
  • Style diversity is limited: Compared to Midjourney, Firefly's output range is narrower. Everything tends toward clean, commercial photorealism
  • Photo editing only in Photoshop: Generative Fill requires a Photoshop subscription on top of Firefly. Standalone web app doesn't have the same capabilities
  • No standalone video generation: Unlike competitors, Firefly doesn't generate video from text prompts. Adobe has video features in beta but they're limited
  • Prompt adherence is inconsistent: Complex multi-part prompts get simplified or ignored. Firefly handles 2-3 elements well, but starts hallucinating with 5+

Final Verdict

Adobe Firefly is the best AI image generator for commercial work, and the most limited for creative exploration. If you're a business, brand, or agency that needs commercially safe AI imagery and already lives inside the Adobe ecosystem, Firefly is the obvious choice. Generative Fill in Photoshop alone saves professionals hours every week.

But if you're an artist, concept designer, or creative explorer who wants to push boundaries, you'll hit Firefly's limits fast. The conservative content policy, stingy credit system, and narrower style range make it less satisfying for pure creative work. Midjourney gives you more range. DALL-E gives you more prompt flexibility. Firefly gives you legal safety.

For most professionals, the answer isn't one tool, it's both. Use Firefly for commercial work and client-facing deliverables. Use Midjourney for brainstorming and creative exploration. The $4.99/month Firefly plan is worth it for Generative Fill alone.
