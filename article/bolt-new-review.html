I Didn't Expect Much From Bolt.new. It Built My App in Under 10 Minutes.

By Alex · Updated May 31, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Hands-on testing over multiple days. Paid plans unless noted. Full methodology on our About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

May 31, 2026 · 6 min read · Coding Tools

I've been burned by "prompt to app" tools before. They demo beautifully and then you try to build something real and it collapses into an infinite spinner. So when someone told me Bolt.new could turn a plain English description into a working web app in minutes, I rolled my eyes.

But I tried it anyway. Five projects, one week. The results surprised me.

The first try — a habit tracker

I opened Bolt.new, pasted a paragraph describing a simple habit tracker: add habits, check them off daily, see a streak counter, and get a weekly summary. Nothing fancy. Click generate.

It ran. The terminal scrolled past — "installing dependencies," "building components," "starting dev server" — and then there it was. A working app. With a real UI. A database. I could click around and add habits.

Seven minutes from prompt to running app. I sat there staring at it, waiting for the catch.

"Well," I told myself, "it's probably a toy. Let me try something real."

Project two — a real use case

My friend Carlos, a freelance photographer in Barcelona, needed a simple client gallery app. Something where he could upload photo sets, share private links with clients, and let them pick favorites. He'd been putting off building it for months because it sounded like a lot of work for something that shouldn't exist yet.

I described it to Bolt.new in about four sentences. Authentication? Check. File upload? Check. Password-protected galleries? Check. The whole thing took 18 minutes. I sent Carlos the link and he couldn't believe it was a real app.

Then he asked me to add payment integration. And that's where things got interesting.

Where it hits the wall

Bolt.new is good at building from scratch. It's less good at extending existing code. When I asked it to add Stripe payments to Carlos's gallery app, it spent three minutes thinking, then produced a component that referenced a different version of the Stripe SDK than what it had installed. The app broke. I tried explaining the error. It apologized and made a different mistake.

After four failed attempts, I gave up and wrote the Stripe integration myself. Twenty minutes of actual coding. The lesson: Bolt.new is a fantastic first draft generator. It is not a production deployment tool.

Same pattern showed up in project three — a simple invoicing tool. The first build was impressive: tables, forms, PDF preview. But when I asked it to handle tax calculations differently per state, it kept defaulting to a generic 8% rate no matter how many times I corrected it.

The real audience for Bolt.new

Priya, a product manager in Bangalore who can't code, described an internal dashboard she needed for QA tracking. Bolt.new made it in 12 minutes. She showed it to her engineering team. They were impressed enough to add some custom features themselves. She never touches the code.

"I've been waiting for this my whole career," she told me. "Now when I say 'it would be great if we had a little tool for this,' I can just make it instead of asking someone."

That's the sweet spot. Not shipping production software. Building throwaway tools, internal dashboards, prototypes, side projects you'd never pay a developer for.

What's left on the table

For project four (a small e-commerce frontend), Bolt.new nailed the product listing page and broke on the checkout flow. It doesn't handle edge cases. It doesn't test its own output. If you need something that works reliably for strangers, you still need a real developer.

The pricing matters here too. Bolt.new costs $20/month for the basic plan, and unlimited generation requires $100/month. For the price of prototyping, that's fine. For shipping production software, you're better off with Cursor or Copilot, which give you more control for similar money.

Project five was the one that proved the point. I asked Bolt.new to remake the first habit tracker from project one — but this time with user accounts, data persistence, and a mobile-responsive layout. It crapped out on the login flow three times before I gave up and just accepted the unstyled version. First impression was magic. Second pass was frustration.

Who should actually buy it

Buy if

You're a non-coder who needs internal tools, prototypes, or MVPs fast. You don't care about code quality — you just want something that works right now.

Skip if

You're building something for paying users, need complex business logic, or want code you can maintain long term. You'll spend more time fixing Bolt's mistakes than writing things yourself.

Bolt.new changed my mind about what prompt-to-app tools can do. But it also reminded me why "can" and "should" are different questions. For the first 10 minutes, it feels like magic. For day two and beyond, it feels like working with an intern who's incredibly fast, doesn't know what they don't know, and never asks for clarification.

That's a useful intern to have. Just don't give them the keys to production.
