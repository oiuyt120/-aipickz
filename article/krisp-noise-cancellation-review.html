Krisp Review: I Didn't Think Software Could Fix Bad Audio. Then I Took a Call From a Coffee Shop.

By Alex · Updated June 3, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Hands-on testing over multiple days. Paid plans unless noted. Full methodology on our About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

I have taken calls from airport lounges, coffee shops, and the passenger seat of a borrowed car. Every time, the first question was always the same: "where are you?"

Not because people were curious about my travel. Because the noise told them I wasn't at a desk.

I never believed software could fix that. Noise cancellation in headphones? Sure. A microphone that only picks up your voice? That's hardware. Software processing the audio after the fact? I assumed it would sound like talking through a pillow.

I was wrong. But not completely.

The Setup

Krisp runs as a system-level audio processor. You install it, pick your input and output devices inside the app, and it removes background noise in real time. No per-app setup. No virtual cables. It just works between your microphone and whatever app you're using.

It costs $8/month or $72/year. There is a free tier — 60 minutes of processing per day. Enough for a few meetings, not enough for a full workday.

I tested it across four scenarios. Same laptop. Same cheap USB microphone. Different locations.

Coffee Shop

Espresso machine hissing, chatter, chair scrapes, a blender running.

Pass: caller heard only my voice

Coworking Space

Open plan with 20 people, phone conversations nearby, keyboard clatter.

Pass: clean on both ends

Home Office (bad)

Street traffic, neighbors drilling, my own HVAC rumble.

Pass: voice crisp, background gone

Windy Balcony

12-15 mph wind, traffic 50 feet away, birds.

Fail: muffled, some wind passed

What Surprised Me

The voice quality. I expected robotic artifacts — that underwater sound that happens when aggressive noise gating kicks in. Krisp does not sound like that.

My voice came through clean. Not studio-quality, but indistinguishable from a normal desk setup. On a test call, my friend asked "did you get a new mic?" He did not believe I was sitting in a coffee shop.

Speaker separation was the second surprise. Krisp removes noise on both ends — your microphone AND your speakers. So if your colleague is on a bad connection with background noise on their end, you hear them cleaner too. That part I did not expect.

Lena, a project manager at a remote-first startup in Berlin, uses Krisp for all her client calls. "I used to panic if my neighbors started drilling. Now it sounds like I'm in a recording booth. I honestly forget it's running until someone mentions how clear my audio is."

What Still Bugs Me

Music. Krisp treats music as noise. If you take calls in environments with background music — a cafe with a playlist, a bar with a jukebox — it tries to remove that too. The result is a weird pumping effect where the music dips in and out as the AI guesses whether it's noise or ambience. It is not good.

CPU usage is noticeable. On a MacBook Air M1, Krisp consumes about 8-12% CPU during calls. That is not nothing on a machine that sips power. On an older Intel laptop, a reviewer on Reddit reported 20-25% sustained usage. Battery life takes a hit.

And the free tier runs out fast. Two back-to-back hour-long meetings and you are done for the day. The paid tier is cheap at $8/month, but it is another subscription.

Who Should Get It

If you take calls from noisy environments and your job depends on sounding professional, Krisp is worth the $8. One client call where you sound polished instead of apologetic covers the annual fee.

Skip if you work from a quiet home office with a decent microphone. You do not need it. Also skip it if you take calls from bars or restaurants with music — the AI will fight the playlist and lose.

Software noise cancellation is real. I did not believe it either. But Krisp handles the hard case: busy coffee shops and open offices — better than I thought possible. It is not magic. It cannot fix bad internet or make you sound like a podcast host. It can make your background irrelevant. And for remote workers, that is half the battle.
