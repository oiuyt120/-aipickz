Perplexity AI Review 2025 — Can AI Search Really Replace Google?

May 24, 20269 min read

A few weeks ago I asked a group of 12 people: "When was the last time you opened Google and didn't hate the experience?" The answers were surprisingly consistent. "Every time I search for a programming error I get 5 blog posts that copied each other." "I spend more time filtering results than reading them." "The first page is all ads."

That's where Perplexity AI comes in. Instead of showing you 10 blue links, it reads the web, synthesizes answers, and cites its sources. It sounds like the obvious next step for search. But does it actually work day-to-day?

I tested it for two weeks across 5 real scenarios. Here's what I found.

What Is Perplexity AI?

Perplexity is an AI-powered search engine. You ask a question, it searches the web in real time, and gives you a direct answer with inline citations. Think of it as "Google, but the AI read the top 20 results for you and wrote a summary."

It comes in two flavors:

Test 1: Quick Fact-Finding

Prompt: "What are the latest FDA-approved cancer treatments in 2025?"

Google experience: You get a mix of press releases, news articles, and a Wikipedia page. You open 5-6 tabs, skim each one, and piece together the answer. Takes about 10 minutes.

Perplexity experience: It returns a structured answer with 8 citations from the FDA website, major medical journals, and news outlets. Each claim has a numbered source. You can click any citation to verify. Takes about 20 seconds.

Winner: Perplexity — for speed and structure.

Real story

Maria is a healthcare analyst who writes monthly reports on oncology trends. Her old workflow: Google, open 15 tabs, read, take notes. She switched to Perplexity after a colleague showed her. Now she asks "What's the survival rate improvement for CAR-T therapies in 2025?" and gets a sourced answer in seconds. "I used to spend 3 hours gathering sources for each report. Now it's maybe 30 minutes. I'm not going back."

But: Perplexity can miss very recent news (same-day breaking stories). Google still wins for pure freshness.

Test 2: Troubleshooting a Technical Problem

Prompt: "PostgreSQL connection pool keeps dropping under load. How do I debug this?"

Google experience: The usual suspects — Stack Overflow, a few blog posts, maybe a Reddit thread. Some are relevant, some are outdated (2018 Postgres 9.6 advice for a Postgres 16 problem). You try a few fixes, one of them works after an hour.

Perplexity experience: It searches documentation, recent forum posts, and engineering blogs. The answer covers connection pool sizing, pgbouncer settings, and kernel-level TCP keepalive tuning. Each point has a source. You can follow up with "What about pgcat vs pgbouncer?" and it picks up the thread naturally.

Winner: Perplexity — the follow-up conversations are the killer feature. You can drill down like you're talking to a senior engineer.

Real story

James, a backend engineer at a fintech startup in Austin, keeps Perplexity open on a second monitor all day. "When I hit a weird database error at 2 AM, I don't want to browse 10 Stack Overflow threads. I want a straight answer with sources I can trust. Perplexity does that." He still uses Stack Overflow for niche community knowledge, but Perplexity is his first stop now.

But: For extremely niche or obscure problems (internal enterprise tools, legacy code nobody writes about), Google + Stack Overflow still wins because the best answer might be buried in a forum thread from 2017.

Test 3: Writing & Research

Prompt: "Write a 500-word LinkedIn post about the future of remote work, supported by recent data."

Google experience: You search for "remote work statistics 2025," find a McKinsey report, a Buffer survey, and a few news articles. You write the post yourself, pulling quotes from each source.

Perplexity experience: It pulls the same data into a structured answer with citations. You can ask follow-ups like "What about hybrid models?" and it adds more sources. Then you write the post yourself — Perplexity doesn't write it for you (and you wouldn't want it to).

Winner: Tie. Perplexity is faster for gathering sources. But the writing is still your job.

Real story

Sarah, a B2B marketing manager at a SaaS company in Denver, uses Perplexity for all her content research. "I used to spend Monday mornings collecting links for the week's content. Now I ask Perplexity and have my sources in 5 minutes. It freed up about 3 hours a week." She still writes the actual content herself — she tried having AI write it once and says "it read like AI wrote it."

Test 4: Shopping & Product Research

Prompt: "Best noise-canceling headphones under $200 in 2025."

Google experience: You get a mix of Wirecutter-style reviews, Amazon affiliate blogs, and random Reddit threads. Hard to tell what's paid vs genuine.

Perplexity experience: It reads reviews from multiple sources and gives you a summary with pros and cons. The citations include Wirecutter, Rtings, Amazon verified reviews, and Reddit discussions.

Winner: Perplexity — but neither is perfect. Product research is fundamentally subjective, and both tools can lead you astray.

Real story

Mike, a freelance video editor in Portland, needed new monitors. He asked Perplexity "best 4K monitor for color accuracy under $600." The answer cross-referenced 12 different reviews. He narrowed it to two options, checked both on Reddit, and bought one. "I definitely had a shorter shortlist than if I'd Googled. But I still checked Reddit before buying."

Test 5: Deep Research (Pro Feature)

Prompt (on Pro): "Do a deep research on the impact of AI coding tools on junior developer hiring in 2025."

Perplexity Pro's Deep Research mode runs multiple searches, reads dozens of pages, and generates a report-style answer that reads like a mini whitepaper.

Result: A 2,000-word report with 25+ citations across news, industry reports, HN discussions, and academic papers. It covered the hiring slowdown, the "CS degree vs bootcamp" debate, and which companies are still hiring juniors.

Google equivalent: You'd spend 2-3 hours reading and synthesizing. Perplexity Pro did it in about 3 minutes.

Winner: Perplexity Pro — Deep Research is genuinely impressive for this use case.

But: The report can miss nuance. One of the sources it cited was a blog post from an AI recruiting company — essentially a press release dressed up as analysis. The citations are transparent so you can catch this, but it's still something to watch for.

Perplexity is not a Google killer. It's a Google alternative for a specific set of tasks — finding facts, gathering research, and troubleshooting technical problems. For those tasks, it's genuinely better. For everything else (breaking news, random browsing, finding niche communities), Google still wins.

  • Who should use Perplexity Free: Researchers, engineers, and anyone tired of scrolling past ads and SEO spam.
  • Who should upgrade to Pro ($20/mo): Power users who hit the file upload limit, want access to GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet, or do deep research regularly.
  • Who can skip it entirely: Casual searchers who find what they need on Google, or people who prefer browsing links over reading AI summaries.
