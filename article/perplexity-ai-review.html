Perplexity AI reads the web for you and gives sourced answers instead of links. The Free plan is genuinely useful for research and technical troubleshooting. Why I Tried It

I've been using Google search for over a decade. Like most people, I've watched it get worse — more ads, more SEO spam, more results pages that look the same. A few months ago I noticed I was adding "reddit" to every search just to get human-written results instead of affiliate content.

Perplexity AI promises something different: instead of showing ten blue links, it reads the web in real time and gives you a direct answer with citations. No scrolling. No ad-cluttered SERPs. Just an answer and the sources it came from.

I tested it for two weeks across five real-world scenarios — quick facts, technical troubleshooting, research, shopping, and deep research. Here's what actually happened.

Day 1: Quick Setup, First Impressions

I signed up for the Free plan. No credit card, no trial gating. Within 30 seconds I was asking questions.

First query: "What are the latest FDA-approved cancer treatments in 2025?"

Perplexity returned a structured answer with 8 citations from the FDA website, major medical journals, and news outlets. Each claim had a numbered source I could click to verify. The whole response took about 20 seconds.

I ran the same search on Google. Result: a mix of press releases, news articles, and a Wikipedia page. To get the same synthesized answer, I'd need to open 5-6 tabs, skim each one, and piece it together myself. That's about 10 minutes of work.

First impression: This is what I thought search would feel like in 2026. The cited answers are a genuine time-saver.

What surprised me: The follow-up conversations. I asked "What about Medicare coverage for these?" and Perplexity picked up the thread naturally, keeping context. Google doesn't have conversations.

Day 3: The Good, The Quirks Show Up

By day three I was using Perplexity for most of my work queries. Two scenarios stood out as genuinely better than Google:

Technical troubleshooting. I hit a PostgreSQL connection pool issue during testing. Prompt: "PostgreSQL connection pool keeps dropping under load. How do I debug this?" Perplexity searched documentation, recent forum posts, and engineering blogs. The answer covered connection pool sizing, pgbouncer settings, and kernel-level TCP keepalive tuning — each point cited. I could drill down with follow-ups like a conversation with a senior engineer.

Google gave me Stack Overflow links, a few blog posts, and some outdated advice (2018 Postgres 9.6 tips for a Postgres 16 problem). Fixing it took about an hour of trial and error.

Writing research. For content research — statistics, expert quotes, recent studies — Perplexity is noticeably faster. A query like "remote work statistics 2025" pulls data from multiple surveys and studies into one structured answer. It doesn't write the content for you (and you wouldn't want it to), but it cuts source-gathering from 45 minutes to about 5.

Where quirks appeared:

  • Breaking news. Perplexity can miss same-day stories. Google still wins for pure freshness.
  • Product research. A query for "best noise-canceling headphones under $200" gave a decent summary with citations from Wirecutter, Rtings, and Reddit. But product quality is fundamentally subjective — the summary can't replace reading real owner reviews. I still opened Reddit before buying.
  • Niche problems. For an obscure error in an internal enterprise tool nobody writes about publicly, Perplexity had nothing useful. Google + Stack Overflow still wins when the answer lives in a forum thread from 2017.

Where It Broke

Not everything worked. A few failure modes worth knowing about:

  1. Source quality varies. In a Deep Research report about AI coding tools and junior hiring, one of the 25+ citations was a blog post from an AI recruiting company — essentially a press release dressed up as analysis. The citations are transparent so I could catch this, but it's something to watch for. Perplexity doesn't filter for source credibility as aggressively as it should.
  2. Context window limits on Free. Long conversations (20+ exchanges) start losing track of earlier context. The model begins to forget what you asked three turns ago. Pro with GPT-4o handles this better.
  3. No good for browsing. Perplexity is a Q&A tool, not a browser. If you want to explore a topic broadly, discover new blogs, or randomly find cool stuff, Google's link-based SERP is better. Perplexity gives you an answer and moves on.

Bottom Line

Perplexity is not a Google killer. It's a Google alternative for a specific set of tasks: finding facts, gathering research, and troubleshooting technical problems. For those tasks, it's genuinely better. For everything else — breaking news, random browsing, finding niche communities — Google stays relevant.

FAQ

Is Perplexity AI free?

Yes, the Free plan has unlimited search queries with basic models and limited file uploads. Pro ($20/month) unlocks GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet, unlimited file uploads, and Deep Research mode.

Does Perplexity actually replace Google?

For specific tasks — fact-finding, research, technical troubleshooting — it's often better. For breaking news, random browsing, and niche community knowledge, Google still wins. Most people will end up using both.

Does Perplexity make up answers (hallucinate)?

Less than raw ChatGPT, because every claim is grounded in web search results with visible citations. But source quality varies — a cited blog post can still be wrong. Always check sources for important decisions, especially in medicine, finance, or law.
