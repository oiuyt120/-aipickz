I Needed a Voiceover for a Client Demo. WellSaid Labs Delivered in 10 Minutes.

By Alex · Updated June 2, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Hands-on testing over multiple days. Paid plans unless noted. Full methodology on our About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

Last Thursday I had a script, a deadline, and the growing realization that my own voice makes me sound like I am reading off a script.

I record myself in my home office. The mic picks up the fridge hum. My pacing is off. I stumble over words, re-record the same line four times, and end up with a file that sounds like a hostage video. I have been down this road before. Every time, I tell myself the next one will be better. It never is.

The project was a product demo for a client. Three minutes of narration. I spent two hours recording, scrapped everything, and spent another hour trying free text-to-speech tools. Google Cloud TTS sounded like a GPS reading a street name. Amazon Polly was flatter than a week-old soda. The demo was due in 72 hours and I had nothing.

That is when a developer friend mentioned WellSaid Labs. He uses it to narrate internal training videos and said the voices are "good enough that nobody asked questions." I signed up for the trial.

What surprised me

The voices are not robotic. That was my first reaction. WellSaid has about 70 voices across different styles — conversational, energetic, professional. Some sound better than others. The best ones manage natural pauses and emphasis. The worst ones still sound like a human who is slightly tired, which is a massive upgrade over most TTS tools.

I picked a voice called "Tyler" (energetic, male) and pasted my script. The first render took maybe 30 seconds. I listened back. The pacing was wrong on sentence three. I adjusted the timing slider, added a pause marker before the key product reveal, and re-rendered. The second pass was usable. I dropped in background music. The whole process, from signup to export, took about ten minutes.

Maria, a training coordinator at a regional hospital network, told me she uses WellSaid for compliance videos. "I used to pay a voice actor $200 for a five-minute script. WellSaid is $49 a month. The voice actors are better. But WellSaid is fast enough that I can update a video the same day policy changes." That trade-off describes the product well.

Where it falls apart

WellSaid cannot handle emotional delivery. If your script needs anger, excitement, or sarcasm, the voice stays flat. It is a pleasant, well-paced flat — but flat nonetheless. Product narration works. Training videos work. A dramatic brand video does not.

The pronunciation editor exists but is finicky. I had a client name that WellSaid kept mispronouncing. I had to type a phonetic spelling. It worked, but I spent ten minutes on one word. For a long script with industry jargon, that adds up.

There is also no mobile app. The editor is browser-only. If you need to tweak a voiceover on the go, you are out of luck. And the export options are limited: MP3, WAV, or embedded player. No SRT captions, no video export. You need a separate video editor to sync the audio to your footage.

Pricing is $49 a month for the Pro plan (unlimited renders, commercial rights) or $99 for the Team plan (collaboration, custom voice cloning). There is a free trial that gives you a few renders with a watermark. The watermark disappears on paid plans.

Who should use it

Buy if you produce training videos, product demos, or explainer content and need passable voiceovers fast. The speed advantage is real. What takes me two hours of self-recording takes ten minutes in WellSaid.

Skip if your content demands emotional delivery, character voices, or creative narration. A WellSaid voiceover sounds like a competent radio host reading your script. That is enough for most corporate use cases. It is not enough for storytelling.

Verdict: WellSaid Labs is the best TTS tool I have used for practical narration work. But it is a replacement for recording yourself, not for hiring a voice actor. I keep it for client demos and internal videos. For anything creative, I still book a real person.
