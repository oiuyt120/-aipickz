I Built Two Online Courses With AI. One Got Sales. One Got Nothing.

By Alex · Updated May 31, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Hands-on testing over multiple days. Paid plans unless noted. Full methodology on our About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

May 31, 2026 · 7 min read

Last month my co-founder bet me AI couldn't build a sellable online course.

"It'll sound like a textbook written by committee," he said. "No voice. No real examples. No one will pay for it."

I disagreed. I figured with the right prompts and enough iteration, AI could produce something people would buy. So we made a bet. He'd build a course the traditional way: topic he knows cold, writes it himself, records his own voice. I'd build one entirely with AI. ChatGPT for the curriculum. ElevenLabs for the voiceover. Canva for the slides.

Thirty days and two courses later, we both learned something. Just not what either of us expected.

The AI-Only Course

I picked a topic I know: using AI tools for small business marketing. Not because I'm an expert, but because I'd lived it for six months running campaigns for local shops. The AI didn't need my experience. I just needed to feed it good prompts.

I told ChatGPT to write an 8-module course on AI marketing for small businesses. Include case studies. Suggest exercises. Create quiz questions. It spat out 40 pages in two hours. I spent another day polishing, generating slides in Canva, and recording voiceover with ElevenLabs.

Day 3. Course was done. I published it on Teachable at $29.

Tom, a freelance designer in Austin, was the first person I showed it to. He read through the first module and said: "This is factually correct. But it reads like someone summarized five blog posts."

He wasn't wrong.

Two weeks passed. Zero enrollments. Not even a free trial.

The AI-Assisted Course

My co-founder's approach was different. Same topic, but he used AI as a tool, not a ghostwriter. ChatGPT helped him structure the outline and suggested research sources. He wrote the curriculum himself. Every module, every example, every exercise. He spent 8 hours recording his own voice, redoing sections that sounded flat.

"I saved maybe 40 hours on research and structuring," he told me. "But the content is mine. The stories are real. The mistakes I talk about actually happened."

His course went live on day 10. Same platform. Same $29 price.

By the end of week two, he had 4 enrollments. One from a complete stranger.

Priya, a marketing consultant in Bangalore who enrolled, said: "I almost bought a different course that was all theory. This one had real examples I could adapt. The section where he showed his failed Facebook ad campaign? That's what I needed to see."

The AI-only course was faster. The AI-assisted course made money. Speed doesn't matter if nobody buys.

What the AI-Only Course Got Wrong

The content wasn't bad. It was boring. Every point was valid. Every sentence was grammatically correct. But it had no texture.

Real examples require real experience. The AI couldn't describe the time a client's Facebook ad got rejected three times because the copy was too vague. The AI never lived through that. It could describe the rejection process, but it couldn't make you feel the frustration.

Courses aren't Wikipedia articles. People pay for perspective, not information. If all you need is information, you'd Google it.

Where AI Helped

The AI-assisted course still used AI a lot. Just not for the core content.

  • • Outlining: ChatGPT turned a messy brain dump into a logical module structure in 10 minutes.
  • • Research: It suggested studies, statistics, and tools my co-founder hadn't heard of.
  • • Slide generation: Canva's AI turned bullet points into clean slides in seconds.
  • • Quiz creation: AI wrote assessment questions that tested understanding.
  • • Email sequences: Automated follow-ups for enrolled students.

That's the pattern. Use AI for the scaffolding. Build the house yourself.

The Verdict

If you want to build a course fast: AI can give you a complete product in 3 days. But don't expect strangers to pay for it. You might get friends and family who want to support you.

If you want to build a course that sells: Use AI for research, structure, and production. Write the content yourself. Record your own voice. Share your actual failures. It takes longer. But 7 enrollments and $203 in 30 days beats 0 and $0.

My co-founder won the bet. But honestly, I'm glad he did. I would have been wrong about the wrong thing.
