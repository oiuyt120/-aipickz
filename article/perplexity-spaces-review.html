Perplexity Spaces Review: I Built Custom AI Assistants for a Week

May 29, 20266 min read

Last Sunday I had a mess of browser tabs. Five docs on AI ethics, three internal research reports, and a dozen PDFs from academic journals. I needed an assistant that actually knew this material — not one that read the internet and guessed.

That's when I tried Perplexity Spaces. It's a feature inside Perplexity AI that lets you create custom research assistants. You give them a name, upload your documents, write custom instructions, and they answer questions using only the knowledge you provide. No guessing from the broader web. No "according to my training data" hallucinations.

I built three Spaces over a week. Here's what happened.

Space 1: The Legal Research Bot

My first test was personal. My friend Priya, a contracts lawyer in Chicago, has 40-page PDFs of regulatory updates she needs to summarize every quarter. I built a Space for her: uploaded the last three regulatory PDFs (about 150 pages total), set the instructions to "Answer only from the uploaded documents. Cite section numbers. If a question can't be answered from the docs, say so."

It worked. Embarrassingly well.

Priya asked it "What are the disclosure requirements for SaaS companies under the new framework?" The Space pulled the exact paragraph from document two, cited the section number, and summarized it in two sentences. She spent maybe 30 seconds setting up the query. Her old process: scanning 150 pages manually, bookmarking relevant sections, writing a memo. That took two hours.

"I don't trust it for final advice yet — I still cross-check the citations," she told me. "But as a first pass? It cuts my prep time by 80%."

Space 2: The Personal Research Vault

I tried something more ambitious. I created a Space for my own writing project — a long-form article about open-source AI models. I fed it 20 sources: blog posts from Hugging Face, technical papers on fine-tuning, Reddit threads from r/LocalLLaMA, and my own notes in a markdown file.

This is where Spaces started to frustrate me.

The knowledge retrieval is good — but not great. When I asked "What's the cheapest way to fine-tune a 7B model on consumer hardware?" it returned correct answers, but it mixed information from a technical paper (ideal setup) with a Reddit thread (someone's janky practical setup) without distinguishing them. The Reddit user's method was creative but had glaring security holes. The Space treated both sources equally.

There's no source weighting. You can't tell it "trust the academic papers more than Reddit." It treats every uploaded file as equally authoritative. For my research, that meant I had to manually check which answer came from which source. That defeats part of the purpose.

Space 3: The Team Wiki

I shared a Space with two colleagues for a collaborative project. We uploaded our project brief, API documentation, meeting notes, and a shared glossary.

Sharing worked smoothly. One link, no login barriers for team members who already had Perplexity accounts. Questions from any of us pulled from the same knowledge base, and the Space remembered the context between sessions — a feature that standard Perplexity search doesn't do well.

Tom, a product manager in London, said: "I used to ping my devs on Slack every time I forgot what an API endpoint did. This Space just tells me. My devs are probably happier too."

But there's a catch: Spaces are limited by Perplexity's search model, which excels at quick factual lookups but struggles with synthesis across multiple sources. When Tom asked "Based on our meeting notes and the API docs, what's the risk of delaying the auth feature?" the Space gave a decent answer but missed a key trade-off that was buried in the meeting notes from three weeks ago.

The Limitations You Should Know

  • No source hierarchy: Uploads carry equal weight. A whispered rumor in a forum post gets treated like a published paper.
  • Shallow cross-referencing: Spaces handles single-document lookups beautifully. Multi-document synthesis is hit or miss.
  • Upload cap: I hit the file size limit with my 150-page regulatory PDF set. Pro plan ($20/month) gives you more, but free tier feels cramped.
  • No versioning: Update a document and the old one is gone. You can't keep multiple versions of the same knowledge base.
  • Lock-in: Once you build a Space around Perplexity's ecosystem, migrating your custom assistants to another platform means starting from scratch. No export option.

Who Is This For?

I'd recommend Spaces to: researchers who need fast fact-checking against their own document set, small teams that want a shared project wiki that actually answers questions, and anyone doing deep-dive investigation on a specific topic with a defined set of sources.

I'd skip Spaces if: you need nuanced synthesis across conflicting sources, your knowledge base is larger than a few hundred pages, or you want a general-purpose AI assistant — that's what regular Perplexity search is for.

Final Verdict

Perplexity Spaces is a genuinely useful feature that's not quite finished. For single-document lookups with citation requirements, it's the best tool I've tested. For complex multi-source research, it needs source weighting and better synthesis — features that feel like they're one or two updates away.

Get it if: you spend hours searching through your own documents every week and want a faster first pass.

Skip it if: your work requires nuanced judgment across conflicting sources. That's still a human job.
