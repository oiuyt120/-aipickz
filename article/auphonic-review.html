Auphonic Review: The AI Audio Tool That Fixed My Uneven Podcast Levels

By Alex · Updated June 5, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Paid Auphonic account (Pay-as-you-go) · 15 episodes processed · macOS/Reaper.

June 5, 2026 6 min read Voice & Audio

Every episode of my podcast sounded different. One week I was whispering into the mic. The next week I was practically shouting. My listeners probably hated me. I know my ears did.

I tried fixing it. Opened every file in Audacity, highlighted the quiet parts, cranked the gain manually. Then normalized the whole track. Then checked the loud parts didn't clip. It worked. It also took about twenty minutes per episode.

Twenty minutes of my life, every single recording. Zoom calls, guest interviews, solo episodes — each one needed the same manual dance. I knew there had to be something better.

I found Auphonic.

What Auphonic does

Auphonic is an audio post-processing platform. Upload a recording. It analyzes the whole thing and adjusts levels to a target loudness standard. That is the headline feature. But there is more below the surface.

It levels your audio. If you leaned away from the mic for a sentence, Auphonic brings it up. If you got excited and blasted the diaphragm, it backs that off. The result is a consistent volume from start to finish.

It filters noise. Not aggressive noise removal that makes everything sound like you are in a padded room. Just enough to clean up background hum, fan noise, and the ambient rumble that cheap microphones pick up.

And it normalizes to broadcast standards. ITU-R BS.1770. That is the loudness spec that radio stations, Netflix, and Spotify all use. Your episode comes out at exactly -16 LUFS or -23 LUFS or whatever target you pick. No guessing. No clipping.

The whole thing takes about as long as the audio file itself to process. A 45-minute episode finished in about 40 minutes on the pay-as-you-go plan.

Where it surprised me

I expected the leveling to work. The demos on the website show dramatic before-and-after comparisons. What I did not expect was how little I had to adjust afterward.

With my manual Audacity workflow, I always needed a human pass. Listen through, find the spots where normalization made things weird, tweak them. With Auphonic, I stopped doing that by the third episode.

The noise filtering deserves special mention. Most noise reduction tools make audio sound hollow. Auphonic's adaptive filter does not. It drops the floor noise by about 6-8 dB without making voices sound like someone recorded them through a pillow. I ran a test where I recorded next to an air purifier. The processed file was cleaner. Not perfect. But way better than I expected.

There is also the batch processing. You can queue multiple files and walk away. I queued five episodes before bed and woke up to finished files. That alone changed my release schedule.

Where it falls short

Auphonic is not cheap for heavy users. The pay-as-you-go plan costs $11 per hour of processed audio. If you publish weekly 45-minute episodes, that is about $33 per month. The $99/month subscription gets you 15 hours. Either way, the cost adds up faster than you think.

The web interface is functional but dated. It looks like a scientific instrument panel. There is no preview mode where you can scrub through the processed audio before downloading. You commit to the full file or nothing.

And Auphonic does not edit. This is not Descript. It will not remove your ums, your awkward pauses, or the part where your dog started barking. It makes what you recorded sound consistent. Everything else is still your problem.

The pricing is the hardest thing to recommend around. For occasional use — say, a monthly interview — $11 an hour is fine. For weekly podcasters on a budget, that recurring cost bites.

Bottom line

Buy Auphonic if

You publish audio regularly and want consistent loudness without spending twenty minutes per episode doing it manually. The time savings add up fast.

Skip it if

You record in a controlled environment with consistent mic technique, or you are happy with your DAW's built-in normalizer. Auphonic shines on messy audio. Clean recordings get less benefit.

What I'd use instead

If you already use a DAW like Reaper or Logic, their built-in loudness meters and limiters can get you close with more manual control. It will take longer per episode but costs nothing extra. For podcasters who want to set and forget, Auphonic is the easiest path.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Does Auphonic work with music or just speech?

A: Auphonic works primarily with speech. Auphonic handles music intros and outros fine as part of a podcast, but a dedicated music mastering tool is better for songs.

Q: Can I try Auphonic for free?

A: Yes. The free plan gives you two hours of processing per month at reduced quality. Enough to test whether it solves your problem before paying.

Q: Is there a desktop app or only the web version?

A: Auphonic has both. The web app processes in the cloud. The desktop app (Mac and Windows) runs locally. The desktop version is faster for large files and does not count against your monthly credit limit.
