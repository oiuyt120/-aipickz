I Wrote SEO Content With Frase for a Week. Here's Where It Shines.

June 3, 2026 7 min read Writing & Content

Last Monday I had a blank editor, a tight deadline, and a topic I knew almost nothing about. The usual routine: open 15 tabs, read the top search results, piece together a brief, then start writing. That first draft usually takes three hours. Frase.io promised to collapse that into one.

I used it for seven days. Every piece of content I wrote for my clients, I ran through Frase. Day one was a disaster. Day seven was something worth paying for.

Day 1: In over my head

Frase starts with a URL. Paste in a competitor's article or a search results page, and it extracts the key topics, questions, and headings into a content brief. The first time I tried it, I pasted a target article and got back 47 related questions. Readable. Grouped by relevance. It felt like cheating.

Then I hit the AI writer. The first output was bland.

Not wrong. Just bland. It read like a textbook summary of the search results, rephrased to avoid plagiarism. No opinion. No angle. Just content that filled space. I rewrote the whole thing from scratch. The brief had been useful, but the AI writing was a disappointment.

Rachel, a content manager for a B2B SaaS company in Austin, told me she had the same reaction. "I was expecting something usable. What I got was a first draft that took me longer to fix than writing it myself would have."

I almost wrote Frase off after day one. Glad I didn't.

Day 3: Where the value is

The brief generator is the real product. The AI writer is the shiny thing they sell you on. But the brief gets you 80 percent of the way there.

Here's how it works for a post on "AI tools for small businesses." I paste the search URL for that query into Frase. It scans the top 20 results and builds a structured outline: the headings competitors use, the questions people search for, the sub-topics that keep appearing. I get a document with sections already laid out, each with suggested word counts and source links.

This alone cut my research time from two hours to 25 minutes. I wrote the outline myself, based on what the data showed. The AI writer stayed off.

Tom, a freelance writer in Melbourne who charges by the word, said Frase changed how he pitches clients. "I send them the brief as part of my proposal. It shows I know the topic. They don't need to know the tool did the research."

Day three was when I realized Frase is a research assistant that can also write. I started treating it as the former.

Day 5: The optimizer

Frase's optimization score is where it pulls ahead of general AI writing tools. You paste your draft and it compares your content against the top-ranking pages. It scores you on keyword usage, topic coverage, and question answering.

I wrote a 1,200-word draft on "chatbot pricing" without Frase's help, then ran it through the optimizer. Score: 42 out of 100. I was missing three sub-topics every competitor covered. I also hadn't answered "how much do AI chatbots cost" directly — a question that appears in the "People also ask" section on Google.

I added those sections. The score went to 76. The draft was better. Not because Frase wrote it, but because it told me what I'd missed.

The optimizer has a blind spot though. It measures keyword coverage, not quality. You can hit a score of 85 by stuffing the right terms into the right headings, and the content can still be mediocre. I tested this. I wrote a deliberately bad paragraph that used all the target keywords. Score: 68. The paragraph was garbage.

Frase optimizes for search engines, not readers. If your draft is already good, it helps you finish. If your draft is weak, it helps you fail faster.

Day 7: The verdict

A week with Frase changed how I research topics. I still write the first draft myself. But I use the brief generator every time. The optimizer is useful for the final pass. The AI writer sits in a corner, mostly unused.

At $15 per month for the Solo plan, it's cheaper than hiring a research assistant. The Team plan at $39 per month adds collaboration features and shared briefs. If you write SEO content regularly and your bottleneck is research, not writing, Frase is a bargain.

But Frase cannot help if you're writing for readers first. The optimizer rewards completeness, not readability. A score of 90 means you covered every keyword, not that the article is worth reading. You still need a human editor for tone, flow, and voice.

Get it if

Your content bottleneck is research. You write for SEO and need to know what your competitors are covering. You want a structured brief before you type a word.

Skip it if

You write creative or opinion content where SEO coverage takes second place. You want an AI that generates publishable drafts without editing. You're on a free budget and can manage with manual SERP analysis.
