I Didn't Expect Much From This Week's AI Tools. Two Proved Me Wrong.

By Alex · Updated May 29, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Hands-on testing over multiple days. Paid plans unless noted. Full methodology on our About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

May 29, 20266 min read

I spent this week testing five AI tools. I went in expecting nothing.

The AI space in late May is a blur of press releases, clone products, and features no one asked for. Every week brings another launch email with the same pitch: "impressive," "next-generation," "the future of work." I've learned to assume nothing will impress me.

This week, two tools broke through that wall. One of them surprised me so much I moved my entire workflow. The other three, useful, flawed, and everything in between.

1. Codeium. The Alternative to Copilot That's Finally Ready

I've been skeptical of Codeium for over a year. Every competitor to GitHub Copilot promises the same thing: faster completions, better context awareness, and lower prices. Most deliver on one of those. Rarely two.

Codeium changed that this year. The current version completes code with context across your entire project, not just the open file. I tested it on a Django app with 40+ files. It suggested the correct model import from a file I hadn't opened in three days. Copilot would have needed a more specific prompt. Codeium just knew.

What surprised me most: the chat feature. You can ask Codeium questions about your codebase in natural language. "Where does authentication happen?" — it locates the auth middleware, explains the flow, and offers to refactor it. I wasn't expecting that from a completion tool.

The catch: It's resource-heavy. On my 16GB laptop, Codeium's background process eats about 400MB of RAM. That's noticeable when you're also running Docker and a browser with 20 tabs.

Mike, a full-stack developer in Chicago, told me: "I switched from Copilot two weeks ago. The completion quality is about the same, but the codebase chat is something I didn't know I needed. I use it more than Google now."

Best for: Full-stack developers, teams with large codebases, anyone tired of Copilot pricing

Price: Free (individual), $15/user/month (Teams)

2. DeepSeek R1. The Open Reasoning Model That Doesn't Gatekeep

I reviewed DeepSeek V3 a few weeks ago and came away impressed by its price-to-performance ratio. But R1 is different. It's not a general-purpose model. It's a reasoning engine.

I gave R1 a logic puzzle that usually trips up GPT-4o: "A bat and a ball cost $1.10. The bat costs $1 more than the ball. How much does the ball cost?" GPT-4o answered $0.10 (wrong, the classic mistake). R1 walked through the algebra step by step and arrived at $0.05. Then it explained why humans get it wrong. That's the difference.

But here's where my skepticism kicked in: day-to-day use. For quick coding autocomplete, R1 is overkill. It takes 15-30 seconds to reason through a response. That's fine for a complex refactoring task. Terrible for "add a semicolon."

What shocked me: the pricing. DeepSeek R1 costs about $0.55 per million input tokens. Compare that to o1 at $15 per million. For the same reasoning quality. That's not a discount, that's a different category.

Best for: Complex debugging, math, logic problems, code review, research analysis

Price: ~$0.55/1M tokens (API), free on chat.deepseek.com

3. ElevenLabs Reader App. Why I Started Reading Again

I'd never considered listening to articles. I read fast, podcasts bore me, and text-to-speech has always sounded like a GPS giving directions. Then I tried ElevenLabs Reader.

The Reader app takes any text, web pages, PDFs, ebooks, news articles, and reads it in ElevenLabs' premium voices. I dropped a 3,000-word research paper into it, picked a voice called "Rachel," and hit play. The result was indistinguishable from a human voice actor reading my paper. Not a robot. Not a monotone. A person.

Two days in, I'd changed my habit completely. Morning news in Rachel's voice over coffee. A product manual during my commute. Even this article, read back to me for editing, catching awkward phrasing I'd missed on the screen.

It's not perfect. The app chokes on heavily formatted content. Tables, code blocks, and multi-column layouts confuse it. And the free tier gives you only 10 minutes of listening per day, enough to test, not enough to replace your reading.

Best for: Commuters, people with eye strain, researchers who digest long PDFs, content editors

Price: Free (10 min/day), $5/month (unlimited)

4. Claude Code. The Terminal Agent I Kept Installed

I'd heard about Claude Code in our last weekly roundup. I wasn't convinced. Another AI coding tool that runs in the terminal? I've tested a dozen of those this year. Most are clever demos that break on real projects.

I forced myself to use Claude Code for one entire workday. No Copilot, no Cursor, no Windsurf. Just Claude in the terminal.

By noon, I'd refactored a Rust module I'd been avoiding for two weeks. Claude read the file, understood the data flow, and proposed a cleaner architecture with proper error handling. It wasn't just writing code, it was reasoning about design decisions. "This function has three responsibilities," it said. "I'd split it into a validator, a transformer, and a writer." I nodded and hit approve.

What keeps me from full adoption: it's slow for small edits. For a one-line change, launching Claude Code, waiting for it to read context, and getting a response takes longer than just typing the line. But for anything that spans multiple files or requires understanding the system, it's the best tool I've used.

Best for: Major refactors, cross-file changes, understanding unfamiliar codebases

Price: Included with Claude Pro ($20/month) or via API

5. Gemini Live. Voice Chat That Doesn't Feel Scripted

I almost skipped this one. Google's previous voice mode felt like talking to a smart speaker that wanted to sell you something. But Gemini Live launched to strong reviews, so I gave it a shot.

First impression: the flow is natural. You talk, it listens, and it responds in real-time, no lag, no "processing" spinner. I asked it to explain quantum computing to me like I was 12. It did, and when I interrupted with a question, it adjusted mid-sentence. That fluid back-and-forth is something no other voice AI does well.

I used it for three brainstorming sessions. "I need a social media strategy for a new AI tool targeting designers. Walk me through it out loud." Gemini Live didn't write a document, it had a conversation. It pushed back on my assumptions, asked clarifying questions, and suggested angles I hadn't considered. That's more than a voice interface. That's a thinking partner.

The downside: it only works well in English. I tested it in Spanish and got confused responses. And it needs a stable internet connection, drop to 3G and the conversation stutters.

Elena, a product manager in São Paulo, told me: "I use it to prepare for stakeholder meetings. I talk through my arguments out loud, Gemini pushes back, and by the time I'm in the meeting, I've already stress-tested my position. It's like having a co-worker who never gets tired of your questions."

Best for: Brainstorming, meeting prep, learning new topics, practicing presentations

Price: Free with Gemini app on Android (coming to iOS)

The Two I Kept

By Friday, two tools were still open on my machine: Codeium and Claude Code. They solve different problems. Codeium for fast daily coding, Claude Code for the heavy architectural work. Together, they cover the spectrum.

DeepSeek R1 stays in my toolkit for hard logic problems but sits unused on normal days. ElevenLabs Reader changed my morning commute but still can't handle technical content well enough to replace screen reading. Gemini Live impressed me but feels like a tool for specific moments, not daily use.

Two out of five stuck. That's a better ratio than most weeks.
