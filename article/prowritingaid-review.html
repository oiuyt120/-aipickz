ProWritingAid Review 2025 — The Grammar Tool That Teaches You to Write Better

May 26, 20267 min read

Grammarly dominates the grammar-checker market. Everyone knows the name, and for good reason — it works, it's polished, and it catches your embarrassing typos before you hit send. But there's a quieter competitor that does some things Grammarly can't touch. ProWritingAid has been around since 2013, and it's built for a different kind of writer: someone who doesn't just want their errors fixed — they want to understand why something is wrong and how to write better next time.

I spent two weeks using ProWritingAid side by side with Grammarly across three types of writing: a marketing email, a blog post, and a short story. Here's what I found.

What Is ProWritingAid?

ProWritingAid is a writing assistant and style editor that goes way beyond grammar checking. It offers 20+ detailed reports on your writing: grammar and spelling, style, overused words, sentence length variation, readability, clichés, sticky sentences, passive voice, pacing, and even dialogue tags for fiction writers. It integrates with Google Docs, Microsoft Word, Scrivener, Chrome, Firefox, and has a desktop app.

Pricing is straightforward: $30/month for Premium (annual plan works out to $10/month), or $12/month for the annual plan. Compare that to Grammarly Premium at $12/month (annual), and it's in the same ballpark. But ProWritingAid offers a lifetime license for $399 — one payment, forever. No subscription. That's rare in 2025.

Test 1: Grammar and Spelling — The Basics

I fed both tools the same deliberately error-filled document: typos, comma splices, subject-verb agreement errors, misused semicolons, and a few British/American spelling inconsistencies. ProWritingAid caught 42 of 45 errors. Grammarly caught 44. Both were excellent for basic hygiene.

The difference was in how they explained the errors. Grammarly says: "Consider a comma here." ProWritingAid says: "This is a comma splice. Two independent clauses joined only by a comma. You should use a semicolon, a period, or add a conjunction." If you're trying to become a better writer, ProWritingAid teaches you. Grammarly just fixes it.

Test 2: Style Reports — Where ProWritingAid Shines

This is ProWritingAid's killer feature. After my blog post, I ran the "Style" report. It flagged 23 sticky sentences (sentences with too many glue words like "the," "and," "of"). It highlighted 7 clichés I'd written without noticing. It showed me my sentence length distribution as a chart — and I could see I was using too many 20+ word sentences in a row, creating a monotonous rhythm.

I ran the same text through Grammarly. It flagged three style issues. Three. Grammarly's style suggestions are basic — passive voice detection and wordiness. ProWritingAid gives you a full diagnostic. For professional editors, academics, or anyone who writes for a living, this is the difference between a quick polish and a deep edit.

Test 3: Fiction and Creative Writing

I pasted a chapter from my novel-in-progress into ProWritingAid. The "Dialogue Tags" report counted every "said," "asked," "replied," and highlighted overuse. The "Pacing" report showed me where my paragraphs were too dense and where I needed scene breaks. The "Emotion" report flagged sentences that lacked sensory detail. I didn't even know I needed an emotion report. It was genuinely useful.

Grammarly doesn't have any of this. It's not built for fiction. ProWritingAid's creative writing features are clearly designed by people who actually write fiction. The attention to craft — not just correctness — is what sets it apart.

What's Missing

  • UI feels dated: ProWritingAid's interface looks like it's from 2018. Grammarly is sleeker, faster, and more polished
  • No email or social media support: Grammarly has a keyboard app and browser extension that works everywhere. ProWritingAid works in Google Docs and Word but doesn't catch errors in Gmail, Twitter, or Slack
  • Slower inline editing: The real-time suggestions in Google Docs have a noticeable lag compared to Grammarly
  • Overwhelming for beginners: 20+ reports is a lot. New writers might find the depth intimidating rather than helpful
  • AI writing features are basic: ProWritingAid added AI sentence rephrasing in 2024, but it's not as smooth as Grammarly's tone suggestions or ChatGPT-powered rewrites

Real Story

Elena is a PhD candidate in linguistics at the University of Melbourne. She writes academic papers, conference submissions, and grant proposals. "Grammarly was fine for my undergraduate essays," she told us. "But for academic publishing, I need more. I need to know if my paragraphs are too dense, if my sentence variety is boring the reader, if I'm using the same transitions in every paragraph. ProWritingAid's reports give me that."

Elena uses ProWritingAid's "Sticky Sentences" and "Sentence Length" reports as a standard pre-submission checkpoint. "My advisor actually noticed my writing improved after I started using it. Not just in fewer typos — my argument structure became clearer. The tool is essentially teaching me to be a better academic writer every time I use it."

Final Verdict

ProWritingAid is the better tool if you want to become a better writer. Grammarly is the better tool if you want your writing fixed quickly and invisibly. They serve different needs, and honestly, they complement each other well — I know writers who use Grammarly for daily email hygiene and ProWritingAid for serious editing sessions.

For fiction writers, editors, academics, and anyone who writes long-form content regularly, ProWritingAid's style reports are genuinely unmatched. The lifetime license ($399) is a steal if you plan to write for more than three years. But if you mainly write short emails, social posts, and quick documents, Grammarly's polish, speed, and broader platform support make it the easier choice.

ProWritingAid is not a Grammarly killer. It's something rarer: a tool that respects your craft enough to teach you.
