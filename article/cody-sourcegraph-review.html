Cody Review: Sourcegraph's AI Knows Your Codebase. That Changes Things.

By Alex · Updated June 5, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Hands-on testing over multiple days. Paid plans unless noted. Full methodology on our About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

Published June 5, 2026 | 6 min read

The pitch sounded too good to be true. An AI coding assistant that understands your entire codebase, not just the file you're editing. Not just the repo name. The actual code.

I've tried enough AI coding tools to be skeptical. They suggest the next line. Sometimes they nail it. Sometimes they hallucinate a method that doesn't exist. The one thing they all have in common is context blindness. Open five files in your project and the AI still treats each one like it's the only file that matters.

Cody is different. Whether that's better depends on what you're building.

What Cody does

Cody is Sourcegraph's AI assistant. It connects to your repo, indexes every file, and answers questions or writes code with full context of your project.

Not "based on the code in this file" context. Real context.

You can ask: "Where do we handle auth?" and it points you to the middleware file, the JWT verification utility, and the route guard — even if those live in three different directories. James, a backend engineer at a fintech startup in Austin, told me he uses Cody for exactly this. "I spend less time grepping and more time writing. That's the win."

Priya, a frontend developer in London who works on a React app with 400+ components, said Cody caught an import cycle during a refactor that would have taken her team two days to debug. "It just pointed at the dependency graph and said 'this is your problem.' I didn't even ask it to."

Where it surprised me

I expected context-aware suggestions. Every AI coding tool claims that now. What I didn't expect was how Cody handles ambiguity.

Most tools give you one answer. If it's wrong, you rephrase. Cody presents multiple approaches, each tied to specific files in your project. "You could implement this in the service layer" — click, it shows you the service layer. "Or you could do it in the controller" — click, there's the controller.

It does not guess. That alone makes it worth having open during a refactor.

There's also the chat interface. It's embedded in VS Code and JetBrains, which sounds basic until you realize how much faster it is than tabbing out to a browser and typing into ChatGPT. The responses reference your actual function names and variables. No translation layer. No "in the context of a typical application" preamble.

But I still won't use it for everything

Cody's biggest strength is also its weakness. It needs your codebase indexed before it works. That takes time. For a small project it's seconds. For a monorepo it can be minutes.

More importantly, Cody targets people who work inside an existing codebase. If you're starting a greenfield project, writing a prototype, or hacking something together from scratch, Cody's deep context is overkill. You don't need understanding of twenty files when you've only written two. A faster, lighter tool like Cursor's inline suggestions gets you there quicker.

Cody also struggles with very new frameworks. My tests on a Bun + Hono project produced suggestions that leaned toward Express.js patterns — the training data's bias showing through. It's accurate on established stacks (Django, Rails, Next.js). Less so on things released in the last 18 months.

And the pricing. Cody Pro is $9/month per user. That is cheap for what it does. But the free tier caps at 500 completions and 50 chat messages per month. As a try-before-you-buy window it works. Beyond that, you feel the cap fast.

Verdict

Buy if you work on a codebase with more than a few files and you're tired of pasting context into ChatGPT. It will save you the grep-to-browser-to-ChatGPT dance. It will catch dependency issues and suggest patterns that match your actual architecture.

Skip if you mostly write greenfield code or work solo on small projects. Cursor, Continue.dev, or even Copilot Free will serve you better for less complexity.

Cody is a tool for teams. That is not a knock. It's the first AI assistant that treats your codebase as a whole thing, not a collection of isolated files. For the right person, that changes everything. For everyone else, it changes the wrong things — and you feel the weight.
