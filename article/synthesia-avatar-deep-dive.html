Synthesia Deep Dive: I Tested Its Avatars on 20 Scripts — and Found the Breaking Point

I didn't think an AI avatar could pass for a real person.

The first few videos proved me wrong. The avatar blinked at the right moments. Its head moved naturally between sentences. The lip sync was tight enough that I stopped looking at the mouth after ten seconds. Which is exactly when you know the tech is working.

Then I pushed harder.

I wrote scripts that required emotional range. I asked the avatar to sound frustrated, then excited, then apologetic. I gave it long technical paragraphs with acronyms and parentheticals. I tested it on 20 scripts across five categories: training videos, product demos, customer testimonials, social media clips, and internal announcements.

Some of these looked shockingly good. Others made the avatar look like an animatronic at a theme park that's been running the same routine for ten years.

The one feature that matters

Synthesia has templates, a script editor, a screen recorder, and a decent library of stock footage. None of that matters if the avatar isn't convincing.

Everything else is furniture. The avatar is the room.

So I focused entirely on that: how good is the avatar, when does it work, and where does it break?

Voice cloning. I uploaded a 3-minute sample of my own voice. Synthesia processed it in about 90 seconds. The cloned voice captured my pacing and tone reasonably well. Not perfect, but far better than the stock voices. The stock British male voice ("James") sounded like a podcast host from 2018. The American female voice ("Emma") was cleaner but had that slight AI warble on long vowels.

Lip sync. This is where Synthesia has improved the most. On straightforward declarative sentences ("Our Q3 revenue grew 12 percent"), the sync was near-perfect. The mouth shapes matched the phonemes. It felt real.

But throw in a tongue twister or a word like "sixth" and the avatar's mouth went into a kind of visual uncanny valley. Not obviously wrong, but slightly off. Like watching a dubbed film where the actor's lips don't quite match the audio track.

Gestures. Synthesia avatars gesture on autopilot. They nod, open their hands, shift their weight. About 70% of the time these looked natural. The other 30%: a hand gesture that came two beats too late, a nod that contradicted the sentence's meaning. It reminded me I was watching a simulation.

Mark, a training manager at a SaaS company in Austin, put it bluntly: "For our internal onboarding videos, nobody cares. They're watching at 1.5x speed with their Slack notifications open. But for the customer-facing demo we were going to put on our pricing page? We had to reshoot it with a real person."

When the avatar works

Short scripts. Simple language. No emotional heavy lifting.

If your script is 60-90 seconds of declarative information ("Here's how to reset your password. Click Settings. Go to Security. Follow the prompts"), Synthesia's avatar is good. I showed three of these videos to friends without telling them it was AI. Two assumed it was a real person.

Internal company videos are the sweet spot. Training modules, policy updates, weekly announcements. The audience is already primed to accept production shortcuts. A slightly robotic hand gesture doesn't matter when the content is "new expense reporting policy effective next Monday."

Priya, a content creator in Bangalore who manages video for a B2B software company, told me she uses Synthesia for rough cuts. "I draft the video in Synthesia, send it to stakeholders for script approval, and then reshoot with a real actor once we know the words are right. It saves me about two rounds of edits per video."

Where it breaks

Emotion is the wall Synthesia hits.

I wrote a script that started upbeat ("We're thrilled to announce..."), shifted to serious ("But we also have to address..."), and ended hopeful ("Here's how we're fixing it"). The avatar delivered all three sections with the same reassuring smile. Tone stayed flat. Energy never changed.

The avatar wasn't bad. The problem was it stayed consistent across emotional shifts. Consistency kills emotional storytelling.

Long scripts (over 3 minutes) also exposed drift. By minute four, the avatar's gestures became repetitive. The same nod-and-hand-spread combination appeared every 20 seconds. The blinking pattern became predictable. Once you notice the loop, you can't unsee it.

And then there's the background issue. Synthesia lets you choose from 50+ studio backgrounds, but the avatar stays in its own layer. If your script mentions pointing at something, the avatar can't point. It just gestures into the void. That's a hard limit of the current tech.

The verdict

Get Synthesia if you need quick internal videos, training content, or rough cuts for stakeholder review. It saves time, and the avatars are good enough for screens that don't demand emotional connection.

Skip if your video needs to persuade, inspire, or handle complex emotional tone. The avatar can't do nuance. It never blinks off-rhythm. It never hesitates before a hard sentence. A real person (even a cheap one from Fiverr) will outperform Synthesia on anything that requires feeling.
