I Needed a Demo Video for a Client. HeyGen Built It While I Made Coffee.

Last Tuesday, I had a problem. A client needed a product demo by Thursday. I can write proposals and build slide decks. I cannot stand in front of a camera and sound normal.

Freelancer timeline: minimum a week. Recording myself: fourteen takes with a delivery that sounded like a hostage reading a ransom note. I needed something in between.

That's when I opened HeyGen — the AI avatar platform I'd seen in newsletters for months but never tried. I expected bad lip sync, a robot voice, and a face that looked like a video game NPC from 2018.

The first video surprised me.

What worked

You paste a script, pick an avatar from about 100 options, choose a voice, and click generate. That's the whole setup. No templates to configure. No voice training required.

Four minutes later, I had a video. "James," business casual with a warm background, read my script. The lip sync was better than I expected. Not quite perfect. There's a micro-delay on hard consonants, a half-frame skip on words like "preparation" and "strategic." But close enough that you stop noticing after ten seconds.

The voice was the real surprise. HeyGen uses ElevenLabs and Microsoft AI voices. The better ones sound natural. Emphasis lands in the right places. Pauses feel intentional, not glitchy.

I showed it to my wife without context. She asked "who's the guy?" Not "what is that thing?" I counted that as a win.

Where it broke

I got ambitious. I wanted the avatar to gesture at a chart or walk across the frame. HeyGen did not cooperate.

The avatars sit in one spot. They talk. They blink. Their hands make small movements — the kind you make when you're on a Zoom call. But they cannot point at anything, cannot interact with objects, and cannot change their expression mid-sentence.

"I use it for first drafts," said Mike, a video producer in Portland who works with B2B startups. "It saves me from booking a studio for a five-minute internal explainer. But I wouldn't send raw HeyGen output to a client. The jaw jitter is visible at standard quality."

He was right. The standard quality setting has micro-jitter around the jawline. Fine for an internal walkthrough. Noticeable if you're presenting to a decision-maker.

I tried the "Studio" quality setting. Better, but the render time jumped from four minutes to eleven. And the avatar lighting still shifted between frames. Same person. Slightly different shadows. A small thing that catches your eye if you're looking for it.

The moment it clicked

Day three. Ninety-second script about a SaaS tool I'm evaluating. I pasted it in, picked "Maya": the avatar with glasses who looks like a real product manager, chose a warm voice, and hit generate.

Four minutes later, I had a video that explained the product clearly. No verbal tics. No awkward pauses. No fourteen takes. I sent it to the client. They said yes to the meeting.

That's the thing about HeyGen. It does not replace a real presenter with emotional range. But it replaces the nightmare of recording yourself twelve times before you get one usable take. For internal demos, quick walkthroughs, and onboarding videos, it's the fastest option I've found.

What still sucks

The pricing is steep. Creator plan costs $24 a month for 10 minutes of video. The Business plan is $72 for 30 minutes. For a one-off demo, that's reasonable. For regular use, it adds up fast.

The script-to-video pipeline also has a ceiling. HeyGen reads your script but does not interpret it. If a line needs a pause for dramatic effect, you have to add that pause manually with a comma and a note in the script. The AI handles plain language well. It handles subtext, irony, or rhetorical questions poorly.

Custom avatars require a recording session. You can create a custom avatar from a 10-minute video of yourself. The result looks more like you than a stock avatar, but the setup kills the speed advantage. At that point, you might as well record yourself.

The verdict

I'll keep the subscription. Not for client facing launch videos or anything that needs emotional weight — the avatars are still visibly synthetic. But for one-off explainers, onboarding walkthroughs, and any situation where "good enough today" beats "perfect in three days," it's the fastest pipeline I've found.

Buy if: you need quick demo or explainer videos and you don't want to be on camera. Especially useful for internal comms, product walkthroughs, and sales prospecting where speed matters more than polish.

Skip if: you need emotional delivery, complex movement, or zero AI tells. Real video or Synthesia's higher-end plans may serve you better.

The skepticism was half-right. HeyGen is not a production studio. But for the one thing I needed — a professional-looking video, fast, without hiring anyone — it delivered.
