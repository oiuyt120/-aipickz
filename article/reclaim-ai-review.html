Is Reclaim AI Worth It? I Tested the AI Calendar Assistant for Two Weeks

May 27, 20267 min read

I've spent a decade blocking time on my calendar manually. Every Sunday night, same ritual: slot in deep work hours, move the leftovers, stare at the gaps, realize I forgot lunch again. I didn't think AI could do better. It's my calendar. My rhythm. My chaos.

Turns out, I was half wrong.

Reclaim AI is a smart scheduling tool that connects to Google Calendar and automatically optimizes your time — blocking focus hours, scheduling tasks, defending breaks, and even tracking habits. I went in skeptical. Two weeks later, I'm still not a true believer. But I understand why people are.

What Changed My Mind

Day one, I connected Reclaim to my Google Calendar. It scanned my existing schedule, noted my recurring meetings, and asked me to set "defended time" — blocks the AI would protect at all costs. I set 9-11 AM as deep work. No meetings, no tasks, no interruptions.

Within an hour, someone booked a meeting at 9:30 AM. Reclaim automatically declined it and sent a polite note suggesting three alternative slots. The meeting organizer picked one of them. No back-and-forth. No awkward "can we reschedule" email.

That was the moment I stopped rolling my eyes.

Reclaim's scheduling link feature is the best I've seen. Unlike Calendly, which shows all your open slots and lets people pick freely, Reclaim considers your priorities. If you have a task deadline that afternoon, Reclaim won't let someone book over your prep time. It's not just showing availability — it's protecting your stated priorities.

Task Blocking — The Feature I Wanted to Hate

Reclaim syncs with Asana, Linear, Todoist, Jira, and Trello. It reads your task list, estimates how long each task will take (you set the duration), and auto-blocks time on your calendar to do them.

I connected my Linear board. I had 14 tasks, roughly four hours of work. Reclaim scattered them across the week in two-hour blocks between my meetings. It left buffer time. It accounted for lunch. It even left a 30-minute gap before my 3 PM standup so I could prep.

The result was a calendar that looked — for the first time in years — like a realistic plan, not a wishlist.

But here's the catch: Reclaim doesn't know when a task is harder than it looks. If you said a feature takes 45 minutes but it actually takes three hours, the AI keeps scheduling new tasks on top of the overrun. There's no "feeling stuck" detection. No "this is taking longer than expected" flag. You have to manually adjust the duration or push things yourself.

For small, predictable tasks — reply to emails, review PRs, update docs — it's excellent. For ambiguous creative or engineering work, it's optimistic to the point of being misleading.

The Habit Tracking Surprise

Reclaim has a feature I dismissed as gimmicky: habit tracking. It blocks time for things like "exercise," "read," "lunch," or "meditate." If something else comes up, Reclaim automatically reschedules the habit to the next available slot instead of just canceling it.

I set a 30-minute "read" block at 8 PM every day. During week one, three evening meetings tried to override it. Each time, Reclaim pushed my reading block to the next morning. I actually read five out of seven days that week. For a feature I thought was useless, it was the one I missed most when I turned it off.

Real Story

Elena Torres runs a 12-person design agency in Barcelona. She manages clients across three time zones and was spending 90 minutes every Monday just scheduling the week. "I used to have a recurring calendar block literally called 'Calendar Tetris' where all I did was move tasks around to make them fit," she told us. "Reclaim eliminated that completely. I set my priorities once, and the AI does the Tetris for me."

The trade-off, she says, is that her team can't see why Reclaim made certain scheduling decisions. "Sometimes a client gets a weird time slot and I can't explain why. I just say 'my AI said this works best.' That's fine for clients I trust. For new ones, it feels impersonal."

The Skeptic's Checklist — What Still Bugs Me

  • Over-optimistic task estimation: The AI takes your word for how long things take. It won't learn that your "30-minute code review" is actually an hour. You have to correct it manually, repeatedly
  • No Outlook or Apple Calendar support: Google Calendar only. If your company uses Outlook, Reclaim is a non-starter
  • Shallow integration depth: Reclaim reads your tasks but doesn't update their status. Finish a task? You still need to check it off in Linear. Reclaim doesn't know you're done
  • Pricey for solo users: The Starter plan is $8/month (one calendar). The Business plan jumps to $25/user/month. For a solo freelancer, $8/month for calendar optimization feels steep
  • No desktop app: It's a web app and Chrome extension. The mobile experience is just the Google Calendar mobile view with Reclaim's overlays — clunky
  • Scheduling conflicts between Reclaim users: If your team also uses Reclaim, it handles cross-calendar optimization well. If they don't, it can't negotiate schedules — it can only decline and suggest alternatives

The Verdict (for Skeptics)

Reclaim is worth it if your calendar is a mess and you're willing to let go of control. The scheduling link alone saves me the awkward "how about Tuesday at 3?" dance. Task blocking is surprisingly good for predictable work. Habit tracking is the feature I'll miss most after writing this review.

But here's what I still won't use it for: creative work, ambiguous projects, or anything where the time estimate is a guess. Reclaim needs certainty to shine. If your workday is full of unknowns, the AI paints a pretty picture that falls apart by Tuesday afternoon.

For founders, agency owners, and remote workers whose calendars are a war zone — Reclaim is worth the $8/month experiment. For anyone whose work is too fluid to fit in neat 45-minute blocks? Keep your manual calendar. You're not missing much.

Tested May 2026 on Reclaim.ai Starter plan ($8/month) connected to Google Calendar and Linear. Features may vary by plan and region.
