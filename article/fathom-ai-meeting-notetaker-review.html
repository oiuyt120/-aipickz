I Was Wrong About AI Meeting Notetakers. Fathom Proved It.

Do you need another AI tool listening to your meetings?

That was my first reaction when someone pitched Fathom. I'd already tested Granola, Otter, and Fireflies. Granola records locally and never joins your video call. Otter transcribes everything in real time. Fireflies auto-joins your calendar events like a silent guest. Each had a strength. None made it past the trial period.

Fathom's pitch sounded identical: join a call, record, generate notes. I almost skipped it.

Then a client call went sideways. I needed the exact wording of something a stakeholder said — and I did not write it down.

The thing that caught me off guard

Fathom does not just give you a transcript. It splits your meeting into labeled sections: topics discussed, decisions made, action items assigned. Fathom pulls each section from the transcript and tags it with timestamps. You are not scrolling through a 45-minute wall of text looking for "when did we agree to ship the landing page." You read six sections and find it in seconds.

That sounds like a small difference. It changes everything.

I tested it on a 30-minute planning call with a developer client. Fathom returned four action items — three correct, one wrong — plus six decisions summarized with timestamps. I forwarded the raw output as our meeting notes. The client replied: "This is better than anything I wrote down."

Jack, a freelance designer in Austin, started using Fathom after I mentioned it. He says he stopped taking his own notes entirely. "I listen now. The notes are better than what I scribbled while half-paying attention."

What breaks

The action item detection is good, not great. Fathom flagged "we should check the competition" as an action item. That is a vague thought, not a task. It missed "I will draft the proposal by Friday" because the speaker used a contraction mid-sentence during a longer turn. You can edit the output. Editing takes about five minutes per 30-minute call — not nothing, but less than re-listening to the whole conversation.

The integrations work — HubSpot, Salesforce, Slack — and the search is fast. But there is no free export option. The free plan keeps your notes searchable inside the app and that is where it stops. Unlimited exports cost $19 a month. Granola offers similar pricing with a different trade-off: it works offline and on your local machine, but it misses the structured sectioning that makes Fathom useful.

And Fathom does not work on mobile. If you take calls on your phone, you are out of luck. That alone might push you toward Otter, which handles mobile calls fine but lacks the clean output structure.

Who should buy it

Buy if you take client facing calls and want structured notes without manual effort. The sectioned output saves real time compared to raw transcripts or self-written notes.

Skip if your meetings are informal and nobody expects follow-ups. Skip if you need mobile support or offline recording.

Verdict: Fathom stayed on my desktop. But I still open Granola for casual internal meetings where I do not need structured output. The tool I reach for depends on the call — which is, honestly, the right setup.
