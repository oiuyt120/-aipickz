I Uploaded 100 Files to ChatGPT Last Month. The Results Changed How I Work.

By Alex · Updated June 2, 2026 · AI Pickz

A month ago I dragged a CSV into ChatGPT. 3,200 rows of freelance income, expenses, and tax deductions. I'd been meaning to organize them for a quarter and kept putting it off.

The chat box said "Analyzing" for about four seconds. Then it showed me a table. Categorized expenses. Monthly trends flagged. One row where a $2,400 charge was accidentally in "miscellaneous" instead of "software."

My accountant charges $150 an hour.

I'd been using ChatGPT for writing, coding, and random questions since late 2023. The file upload button sat in a corner of the chat box. I figured it was for screenshots.

I spent a month testing what happens when you feed ChatGPT files. Here's what I learned.

What I tested

Five types of documents over 30 days. CSV and Excel spreadsheets. 50-page PDF reports. Screenshots of dashboards and whiteboards. Audio recordings of meetings (transcribed first). Source code from a project I inherited at work.

I created a folder on my desktop called "chatgpt-input." Every day I dropped something in.

Where it nailed it

CSVs are ChatGPT's strongest format. Drop a spreadsheet and it immediately understands the columns, detects data types, and asks clarifying questions. "I see a column labeled Q4 Variance. Is this percentage or dollar value?" That question alone made me trust the output more than a tool that silently produces charts.

Mark, a real estate analyst in Phoenix, told me he uses this for property data. "I upload county tax records — 10,000 rows sometimes. ChatGPT finds patterns in 30 seconds that would take an afternoon in Excel." I'd started doing the same thing with my own datasets.

PDFs are hit or miss. Text-heavy documents with clean formatting work well. I uploaded a 47-page SaaS contract. It extracted termination clauses, renewal terms, and pricing tables accurately. But scanned PDFs or docs with complex tables lose formatting. A 12-page market research report with color-coded charts came back with wrong numbers in three places.

The best surprise was code analysis. I dropped a folder of Python scripts into the chat, about 20 files and 4,000 lines. It identified duplicate functions, suggested refactor targets, and generated unit test stubs. Not production-ready code. But as a starting point for a review? Significantly faster than manual.

Where it broke

Numbers get hallucinated. That's the biggest problem and the one nobody talks about enough.

I uploaded a PDF with a table showing "Total: $847,293" at the bottom. ChatGPT read it as "$847,200." Close enough for a summary. Wrong for a legal document. I caught it because I knew to look. If I'd copy-pasted that into a report, I'd have published an error.

These are not edge cases. They happen in maybe 1 in 5 files depending on formatting complexity. The tool is exceptional at extracting meaning. It is not exceptional at extracting exact numbers.

Large files are another limit. The free tier tops out around 50 pages per upload. Plus handles bigger — I pushed a 100MB research PDF through — but response times slow to 30-60 seconds per question. Fine for occasional use. Annoying when you're iterating.

Audio transcription only works if you pre-process. ChatGPT accepts audio files but doesn't reliably transcribe long recordings. I had to run meeting recordings through Whisper first, then drop the transcript in.

The verdict

ChatGPT's file analysis is the most underrated feature in AI right now. Not because it's flawless. But because it turns a general-purpose chatbot into a domain-specific analyst for whatever you throw at it.

I use it weekly now. Tax documents. Contract reviews. Data cleanup. Code inspection. Each use case saves me hours of grunt work.

But I never trust the raw numbers. I verify everything. The real benefit is speed — finding the pattern, extracting the structure, building the first draft. Not skipping the final check.

Buy if: you deal with spreadsheets, contracts, or code regularly and want a quick second pair of eyes.

Skip if: accuracy is non-negotiable on the first pass, or you're handling sensitive documents that shouldn't leave your machine.

After a month of testing, I'm not going back to manual data extraction. But I'm also not trusting a single number without verifying it first. That tension — between speed and accuracy — is the honest truth about ChatGPT's file analysis.
