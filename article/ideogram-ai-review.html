Ideogram Review: I Needed AI Images With Text. Nothing Else Worked.

By Alex · Updated June 3, 2026 · AI Pickz

Every AI image generator can make a gorgeous photo of a coffee cup on a wooden table. None of them can spell.

I run a newsletter. Every edition needs shareable quote cards: a bold statement over a striking image. The idea is simple. Take a quote, slap it on a clean background, and make it look like it belongs there.

Midjourney makes beautiful backgrounds. But ask it to render the words "AI isn't coming for your job" and you get something that looks like a ransom note. Letters overlap. Characters morph into squiggles. The word "your" becomes "you r."

DALL-E handles short words okay. "Hello" works. "AI Tools" works. But push past four words and the letters start drifting. It's like watching someone try to hold a sign in a strong wind.

I needed something that could handle a full sentence.

The Starting Point

Priya, a social media manager in Toronto who runs campaigns for six clients, spends three hours every Monday creating quote cards. She tried Midjourney for two weeks. "The images were stunning," she told me. "But every single text attempt looked like a six-year-old wrote it with fingerpaint."

She switched to Canva. Canva works. But finding the right stock photo, matching the font, adjusting the layout. That's another two hours. She wanted one tool that could do both: generate a scene and add clean text.

I had the same problem. So I went looking for the tool that could solve it.

Trying the Obvious Options

Midjourney v7 is incredible at style. Give it "vintage travel poster" or "minimalist product shot" and it delivers something you'd hang on a wall. But text rendering? Midjourney treats letters as visual patterns, not language. It draws the shape of an "A" without understanding that an "A" should be distinguishable from an "H." The result is decorative gibberish.

Adobe Firefly is better. Its Generative Fill can create scenes with embedded signage: a coffee shop with a chalkboard menu. But you can't give it specific words and expect them to come out right. The sign says "SPECIAL" but reads "5PEC1AL." Close. Not close enough for client work.

DALL-E through ChatGPT Plus handles short phrases. "Grand Opening" on a banner? Fine. "Grand Opening This Saturday — 20% Off Everything"? The numbers go first. Then letters migrate. The "20%" ends up sideways.

Ideogram: The One That Handles Text

Ideogram was built by former Google Brain researchers who saw the same gap I did. Every image generator could make pictures. None could make pictures with words that made sense.

Their approach is straightforward: train the model to treat text as a first-class output, not an afterthought. The architecture uses a Mixture-of-Experts design where one expert specializes in typography and layout.

The difference is immediate. I typed "A dark neon sign reading 'AI Pickz' in electric purple on a brick wall." The first result had clean, readable text. Every letter in place. The apostrophe between "Pick" and "z" was even correct, something most models get wrong because they don't understand apostrophes.

I tried longer prompts. "A motivational poster with the words 'The best time to start was yesterday. The second best time is now.' in bold white serif font against a dark mountain landscape." The text was crisp. No drift. No missing letters.

Priya tested it on her quote cards. "First attempt. One prompt. Done. I stared at the screen for a minute because I didn't believe it worked."

The Catch

Ideogram nails text. But the rest of the image is not as polished as Midjourney. The lighting is flatter. The compositions are simpler. If you compare a Midjourney landscape next to an Ideogram landscape without text, Midjourney wins every time.

Ideogram compensates with strong prompt understanding. You describe a scene and it follows the instructions better than almost any other generator. But artistic depth is not its strength.

The free tier gives 10-25 generations a day with a watermark. The Plus plan is $20/month for up to 500 fast generations and no watermark. For a social media manager producing quote cards four times a week, the free tier is borderline enough. For anything more, you'll need to pay.

There's also no API. If you want to integrate Ideogram into an automated workflow, you can't. It's a web app and a Discord bot. That's it.

Who Should Use It

If your AI images need to include words that people can read — blog thumbnails, social cards, posters, signage mockups — Ideogram is the only tool that delivers. Midjourney looks better. DALL-E is more accessible. But neither can do what Ideogram does.

If you need pristine landscapes or photorealistic portraits without text, stick with Midjourney.

If you need both beautiful scenes with readable text, you'll end up using two tools. That's the honest answer. Ideogram for the text layer. Midjourney for the background. It's not elegant. It works.

Verdict

Edge goes to: Ideogram for text-in-image generation. Nothing else comes close. But Ideogram's general image quality still trails Midjourney's by a noticeable margin. For quote cards, social posts, and signage mockups, it's the right tool. For fine art or photorealistic scenes, it isn't.

Buy if: you need AI images with readable, clean text. Blog thumbnails, social media cards, posters, any use case where the words matter as much as the picture.

Skip if: you only need beautiful images without text. Midjourney or DALL-E will do it better at the same price.
