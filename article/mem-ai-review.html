I Tried to Replace Notion with Mem. The Results Surprised Me

May 28, 20266 min read

I thought AI-powered note-taking was a gimmick.

Notion already works. You build your database, set up your views, and glue everything together with formulas and linked databases. It takes effort, but the result is a custom knowledge system that actually fits your brain.

Then I heard about Mem. An AI-first notes app that organizes itself. No folders. No databases. Just write, and the AI figures out connections, surfaces related notes, and builds a knowledge graph for you.

Sounded like feature-bloated hype. But I was curious. So I did something drastic.

I spent two weeks trying to live entirely in Mem — migrating my active projects, research notes, and daily journal from Notion. Here's what happened.

Notion: Powerful, But You Have to Build It

Let me be clear about one thing. Notion is still the best tool for structured knowledge management. If you need a CRM, a project tracker, a wiki, and a database — all in one app — nothing else comes close.

"I've spent over 200 hours building my Notion workspace," said Priya, a product manager in Bangalore who runs a team of 12. "Every view is custom. Every database is linked. It's perfect — but it took me six months to get there."

That's the trade-off. Notion gives you total control in exchange for your time. The more complex your setup, the more maintenance it requires. A database schema change means updating every view. A new workflow means building a new template from scratch.

Mem takes the opposite approach.

Mem: Less Control, Zero Setup

Open Mem and there's a single text field. That's it. Type something, hit enter, and Mem saves it. No folder selection. No database picker. No "where should this go" decision.

The AI handles organization in the background. It links related notes automatically. It surfaces "collections" — AI-generated groupings of related content. It serves you a daily digest of notes you might want to revisit.

This is brilliant for capture. I imported a folder of 47 research PDFs into Mem, and within minutes it had extracted key points, linked them to existing notes, and suggested connections I hadn't made. The AI identified that three separate notes about "async communication" actually belonged together as a collection.

"I was drowning in bookmarks and half-written ideas," said Tom, a freelance UX researcher in Berlin who works on 4-5 projects simultaneously. "Mem collects everything and the AI surfaces what matters. I went from 12 open tabs to one app."

But capture is one thing. Retrieval is another.

Where Mem Fails (and Where Notion Wins)

Search is unreliable. Notion's search is basic but predictable. You type a word, you get results. Mem's AI-powered search tries to be smart — it returns "related" results based on semantic similarity, not exact matches. Sometimes that's incredible. Other times I'm searching for "budget spreadsheet" and getting notes about "cost optimization strategy" from a completely different project. Useful? Occasionally. Exhausting when I just want the exact file I wrote yesterday.

No tables or databases. This is the killer. Notion's power comes from databases. Mem has none. If your workflow depends on sorting, filtering, or calculating data — a project tracker, a client CRM, a content calendar — Mem can't do it. Period.

The mobile app lags. The iOS and Android apps are functional but slow. Taking a quick note takes 5-10 seconds of loading. The AI features that make Mem special on desktop are reduced or missing on mobile. I found myself emailing notes to myself when I was on the go.

Export feels like a trap. Mem supports export to Markdown, but the linked note references don't survive. Your beautifully connected knowledge graph becomes a folder of disconnected .md files. That's fine for a backup. Not fine if you ever want to migrate somewhere else.

The Verdict: Two Apps, One Workflow

After two weeks, I didn't fully replace Notion. But I also didn't go back to using Notion for everything.

Here's what I settled on: Mem for capture and brainstorming. Notion for structure and execution.

Ideas go into Mem first — no friction, no decision-making about where to file them. The AI connects the dots. Once a project solidifies and needs real organization, it moves to Notion with a proper database structure.

Get it if: you take a lot of notes but struggle to organize them, or you're the kind of person who has 50 browser tabs full of "things to read later."

Skip it if: your workflow runs on spreadsheets, databases, and structured data. Mem will frustrate you within a day.

Mem isn't a Notion killer. It's something rarer — a genuinely new way to think about notes, even if it only solves half the problem.
