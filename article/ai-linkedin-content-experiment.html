I Used AI to Write LinkedIn Posts for 30 Days — One Approach Got Clients, the Other Got Engagement

By Alex · Updated June 1, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Experiment conducted over multiple weeks in 2026. All tools and services at free or trial tiers. Full methodology on my About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

Day one. Blank screen. Zero ideas. I typed into ChatGPT: "Write a LinkedIn post about AI tools for productivity."

It spat out something polished. Inspirational. Vaguely about "the future of work." I posted it without changing much. It got 47 likes. My best post in months.

And exactly zero messages from potential clients.

That was the first two weeks: vanity metrics, no business outcomes. Then I switched approaches. The second two weeks looked completely different.

Approach A: The lazy method

I gave ChatGPT a vague prompt about my industry and let it write the post. I'd skim it, maybe swap a sentence, and hit publish.

The output was never bad. That was the problem.

It was safe. Generic. The kind of post that says "AI is transforming how we work." Technically correct, instantly forgettable. It read like someone who learned about my job from a Wikipedia summary, not someone who actually did it.

Anna, a marketing manager in Chicago who runs her own freelance practice on the side, told me she recognized the pattern immediately. "I can tell within three sentences if someone used AI without editing it. The sentences are all the same length. Every paragraph makes one clean point. Real people write messier."

The likes kept coming because the posts were inoffensive and well-structured. But nobody reached out. Nobody asked about my services. The algorithm rewarded the content, but the content didn't reward me.

Approach B: The method that worked

Week three. I changed one thing: instead of asking AI to write, I asked it to react.

I'd write a raw paragraph myself. Two or three sentences. A real opinion about something that happened that day, like a client rejecting my approach, a tool crashing mid-demo, or a conference talk that made me rethink my strategy. Then I'd paste it into Claude and say: "Make this clearer but keep my voice. Don't add anything that isn't here."

The difference was immediate.

"The posts started sounding like actual conversations instead of LinkedIn articles," said James, a SaaS sales rep in Denver who runs a side consulting practice doing workflow automation. "I tried the same thing after you mentioned it. The engagement dropped by half at first, because the posts had rough edges. But the DMs? They doubled."

That matched my experience exactly. My "lazy method" posts averaged 35-50 likes and 2-3 comments. My "react method" posts averaged 18-25 likes but 8-12 comments and (crucially) 1-2 LinkedIn messages per post from people asking about my work.

One of those messages turned into a $1,200 consulting engagement. A second became a recurring monthly retainer at $400.

Over 30 days: $1,600 in client work from AI-assisted LinkedIn posts. Not enough to quit anything. But enough to prove the approach works when you do it right.

What the AI was good at

Editing, not creating. Tightening, not expanding. Taking a messy opinion and making it readable without stripping the personality out.

It was also great at titles. I'd write the body first, then ask Claude for three headline options. About half the time, the AI's suggestion was better than mine. Shorter. More specific. More likely to make someone stop scrolling.

What it couldn't do: have an original take. Every time I let the AI write from scratch, the post felt like it was written by a committee that wanted to offend nobody.

The honest verdict

AI for LinkedIn content works. But only if you do the hard part first.

The hard part is having something to say. An opinion. A story. A specific experience that only you had. The AI can polish that into something publishable in 60 seconds. It cannot invent it.

Do this: write your raw thought first. One paragraph. No editing. Then paste it into an AI and ask for a clarity pass and a headline suggestion. Post the result.

Don't do this: ask the AI to "write a LinkedIn post about X" and publish whatever comes out. Your audience can tell. Worse, your potential clients can tell.

Five hours a week on this approach brought in $1,600 in its first month. I'd call that a solid start, but only because I stopped treating the AI like a writer and started treating it like an editor.
