HuggingChat Gave Me Access to 6 AI Models for Free. Only One Surprised Me.

By Alex · Updated June 4, 2026 · AI Pickz

How we tested: Hands-on testing over multiple days. Paid plans unless noted. Full methodology on our About page.

Disclosure: Some links are affiliate links. We may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

June 4, 2026 6 min read Chatbots

Most AI chatbots give you one model. You use what you get, and you never find out if something else would work better.

HuggingChat does the opposite. A dropdown menu with six models. Llama 3, Mistral, Cohere, Qwen, DeepSeek, Phi. Switch mid-conversation. Free. No credits. No limits I could find.

I tested every model on the same three tasks to see which one you should use.

The test setup

Three prompts. Same wording, every model. Write a Python script to parse a CSV. Explain quantum computing to a 12-year-old. Draft a cold email for a freelance pitch.

No follow-ups. No chaining. First response is what you get.

Llama 3 (Meta)

The Python script worked on the first try. Clean, commented, no syntax errors. When I asked for error handling, it wrapped the whole thing in try-except without me specifying how. That kind of foresight matters when you are pasting code straight into production.

The quantum explanation was accurate but dry. It used the right analogies but delivered them like a textbook paragraph. Not bad. Just not engaging.

Cold email was fine. Polite. Generic. "I have followed your work and think I could contribute." Would it get a reply? Maybe. Would it stand out? No.

Mistral

The quantum explanation was the best of the six. It used flipping two coins at once as the core analogy. Simple. Visual. I understood superposition better after reading it than I did from any textbook explanation.

The Python script worked but was verbose. Twice as many lines as Llama for the same output. Readable, but wasteful.

The cold email was too formal. "I am writing to propose a mutually beneficial arrangement." Nobody sends that and gets work.

Cohere

Won the cold email category. Short. Direct. "Hi [Name], I have been following your work on [Project]. I think I can help with [Specific Task]." I would send that. I would probably get a reply.

The Python was functional but minimal. No error handling. No comments. It assumed I knew what the code did.

Quantum explanation was good but leaned too hard on metaphors. "Imagine a coin spinning in the air" works once. Doing it for every concept gets tiring.

Qwen

Competent at everything. Excellent at nothing. The safe choice that nobody picks.

The Python script worked but had no edge cases handled. The quantum explanation was textbook-accurate and forgettable. The cold email was grammatically correct and emotionally flat. "I would be happy to help with your upcoming projects."

If Qwen were a person, they would say "fine" when you ask how their day was, and mean it.

One thing I noticed while running these tests: the models disagreed on each other. I asked Mistral why Llama 3 handled the CSV parsing more efficiently. It explained that Llama had better training data for Python. Then I asked Llama the same question about Mistral's quantum explanation. It said Mistral's alignment training prioritized clarity over comprehensiveness. The models were essentially peer reviewing each other. I did not expect that. It is not a feature. It is just an interesting side effect of comparing outputs side by side.

DeepSeek

This was the surprise. The Python script was not just correct but clever. It used list comprehensions and generator expressions where the others used loops. When I asked for a rewrite, it understood what I wanted, not just what I typed.

The cold email was the second best after Cohere. Slightly less direct but warmer. "I have been following the launch of [Product] and noticed you might need help with [Skill]. Here is how I would approach it." That is a real email from a real professional.

Tom, a junior developer in London who uses HuggingChat to compare coding outputs, told me DeepSeek is his go-to. "I run the same prompt through three models. DeepSeek consistently picks the most elegant approach. Llama is safer. But when I want a solution that makes me think 'why did I not see that,' DeepSeek wins."

Phi (Microsoft)

The Python script had a syntax error. Missed a colon on a function definition. The quantum explanation was accurate but confusing. Too many concepts introduced at once.

Phi is the smallest model here and it shows. It is impressive that it runs at all. But for real work, skip it.

The hidden value

The feature I kept coming back to was not any single model. It was testing the same question across three models in one session.

Priya, a data scientist in Bangalore who works with NLP pipelines, uses HuggingChat for exactly this. "When my model gives bad output, I want to know if the approach is wrong or the model is wrong. I paste the same prompt into Llama and Mistral. The one that gets closer tells me how to adjust my approach."

That is the real use case. Not finding the best model. Using the comparison as a diagnostic.

Where it falls short

No memory. Switch from Llama to Mistral and the conversation restarts. You cannot build context across a session. The UI is basic. No markdown rendering in some models. No image upload, no file analysis, no web search by default.

Speed varies. Llama and Mistral are fast. DeepSeek is slower. Qwen and Phi lag during peak hours. You share a GPU cluster with everyone else.

The models get rotated. HuggingChat updates the available list periodically. A model you like today could be gone next week. No warning. Just gone.

The verdict

Use it for:

Testing open-source models without signing up for six accounts. The model switcher saves real time if you evaluate LLM outputs regularly. Priya's diagnostic use case is worth the bookmark alone.

Skip it for:

Anything that needs memory, file upload, or reliability. HuggingChat is not a ChatGPT replacement. It is a testing ground with a chat interface. Treat it like one.

Llama 3 is the safest choice. Mistral for creative writing. DeepSeek for coding. Cohere for professional communication. Qwen if you want to be disappointed by the middle of the road. Phi if you enjoy debugging syntax errors.

None of them replace the paid tools. But they are all free, all in one place, and only one tab away. That alone is worth more than most AI subscriptions I have tested.
